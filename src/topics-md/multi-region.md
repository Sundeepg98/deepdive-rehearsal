## Thesis

Multi-region is running your system across geographically separate regions for three distinct reasons --- **disaster recovery** (survive a whole-region outage), **latency** (serve each user from a nearby region), and sometimes **data residency** (keep data inside a jurisdiction) --- and it is dominated by two coupled decisions: the **topology** (active-passive: one region serves while another stands by for failover, versus active-active: multiple regions serve simultaneously) and **how data crosses regions** (asynchronous replication with a lag and a data-loss window, versus synchronous with a latency cost). Those two choices determine your failover behavior (**RTO/RPO**), your consistency story, and your exposure to the hardest problem --- **split-brain**, when regions cannot see each other yet both believe they are in charge. It is hard because the speed of light makes cross-region coordination expensive (tens to hundreds of milliseconds per round trip), so you cannot just "run the same system in two places" --- you must decide what is replicated how, which region owns which data, and what happens when they diverge.

## Sub

**Why: survive a region outage, cut user latency, meet data residency** -> **topology: active-passive (a standby you fail over to) vs active-active (all regions serve)** -> **cross-region data: async (a lag = your RPO) vs sync (a latency tax), with a single owner per datum to avoid conflicts** -> **zoom out** to RTO/RPO, failover mechanics and traffic routing, split-brain prevention, and the cost of cross-region coordination that makes multi-AZ the right answer for most systems.

## Spine

- **Multi-region buys disaster recovery, latency, and residency --- and each pulls the design differently** --- surviving a whole-region failure wants a standby you can promote; low latency wants active-active with users served locally; data residency wants data pinned per jurisdiction; knowing *which* you are solving for is the first decision because they trade against each other.
- **Topology is active-passive versus active-active** --- **active-passive** (one region serves, a warm/cold standby fails over) is simpler and avoids multi-writer conflicts but wastes the standby and has a failover delay; **active-active** (all regions serve) gives low latency and no idle capacity but forces you to handle concurrent writes and conflicts across regions.
- **Cross-region data is the hard part because the speed of light is fixed** --- synchronous cross-region replication is slow (tens-to-hundreds of ms added per write), so you usually replicate **asynchronously**, accepting a replication lag that is a **data-loss window (your RPO)** on failover; and you decide which region **owns** which data so writes do not conflict.
- **Split-brain is the failure you design against, and RTO/RPO are the numbers you commit to** --- when regions cannot communicate but both keep accepting writes, they diverge irreconcilably; you prevent it with a **single writer per datum, a quorum, or fencing**, and you state **RTO** (how fast you recover) and **RPO** (how much data you can lose) explicitly rather than hoping.

### SDE2 | what multi-region is

What does it mean to run multi-region, and why would you?

Running multi-region means deploying your system across two or more geographically separate cloud regions (e.g. us-east and eu-west), each a full set of infrastructure, rather than in one. There are three distinct motivations: **disaster recovery** --- a whole region can fail (a natural disaster, a large-scale outage, a fiber cut), and running in a second region lets you survive it (fail over and keep serving); **latency** --- users far from your single region see high latency, so serving them from a nearby region cuts round-trip time dramatically (a user in Europe hitting a US-only service pays ~100ms+ each way); and **data residency** --- laws (GDPR, data-sovereignty rules) may require certain data to physically stay within a country/region, forcing per-region data placement. These motivations are different and pull the design in different directions --- DR wants a standby you can promote, latency wants all regions actively serving, residency wants data pinned per region --- so the first thing to establish is *which* problem you are actually solving, because the right topology and data model depend on it. Multi-region is powerful but expensive and complex (you are running and coordinating multiple copies of everything across high-latency links), so it is justified by a real need for one of these three, not adopted by default.

Follow: Same product, but the goal is latency instead of DR. Concretely, what changes?
Almost everything below the deployment diagram. For **DR**, the second region only has to be able to *become* the system later --- it never serves, so there is exactly one writer, the consistency story stays trivial, and replication exists purely for **recovery**. For **latency**, the second region has to **serve now**, which forces three new things: local **reads** everywhere (so every region needs a current-enough replica), a **write story** (either per-datum ownership with cross-region write routing, or true multi-master with conflict resolution), and **always-on geo-routing** rather than a health-checked failover switch. So the same two regions become a different system: DR replicates for *recovery*, latency replicates for *serving*, and only the latency version makes you confront ownership, conflicts, and read-your-writes. That is why naming the motivation first is not a formality --- it decides whether you have a distributed-data problem at all.
Follow: What is the cheapest thing that gets you most of the latency win without going multi-region at all?
A **CDN / edge layer** in front of a single region. Most of what a distant user feels is (a) TLS and TCP handshakes --- several round trips before a byte of content moves --- and (b) reads of cacheable content. An edge PoP terminates the connection near the user, so the handshakes cost ~5ms instead of ~300ms, and cached responses never cross the ocean at all. You keep **one** backend region and pay none of the multi-region tax --- no replication, no conflicts, no failover, no split-brain. You only need genuine multi-region for latency when the *uncacheable, personalized, or write* path is the bottleneck. So the honest ladder is: **CDN/edge, then read replicas, then multi-region** --- the first two capture most of the perceived latency for a fraction of the complexity, and reaching for multi-region before them is a very expensive way to fix a handshake.
Senior: Naming **which of DR, latency, or residency you are solving** --- and knowing that only the latency case forces an ownership/conflict story at all --- is what separates a scoped design from someone who reflexively draws three regions.
Speak: Refuse to design until you know why: **"which are we solving --- surviving a region loss, latency for a global user base, or residency? They pull the design apart."** Then note this is not multi-AZ, and that a CDN buys most of the latency for none of the complexity.

### SDE2 | region vs availability zone

What is the difference between an availability zone and a region, and why does it matter for resilience?

A **region** is a broad geographic area (e.g. us-east-1) that contains multiple **availability zones (AZs)** --- physically separate data centers within that region, each with independent power, cooling, and networking, but close enough to be connected by low-latency links (single-digit ms). The distinction matters enormously for resilience because **multi-AZ and multi-region protect against different failures**. **Multi-AZ** (running across AZs within one region --- the standard, and often nearly free) protects against a single data-center failure: if one AZ loses power or floods, the others in the region keep serving, with negligible added latency because AZs are close. **Multi-region** protects against a *whole-region* failure (a regional outage, a disaster affecting the entire area, a region-wide control-plane failure) --- which multi-AZ cannot, since all AZs are in the same region. The key insight for interviews: **most systems only need multi-AZ**, which is cheap and low-complexity and handles the common failure (a data center going down), while multi-region is a much bigger investment justified only when you genuinely need to survive an entire region disappearing, or need low latency in distant geographies, or have residency requirements. Confusing "we run in three AZs" with "we are multi-region" is a common and consequential mistake --- they are different blast radii.

Follow: If multi-AZ already survives a data-center failure, what is actually left that only multi-region protects against?
The **correlated, region-wide** failures --- the ones that take every AZ at once, which is precisely what AZ independence does not cover. AZs are independent in *infrastructure* (power, cooling, network) but they still share the region's **control plane** and its **regional services**: a failure in the region's control plane, its identity/metadata layer, or a shared regional service can degrade or block all AZs simultaneously, and historically the large cloud outages have looked much more like that than like a single data center losing power. Add region-wide **network/routing** failures, a **bad deployment or config** rolled out region-wide (a self-inflicted blast radius that respects no AZ boundary), a disaster spanning the metro, and any **region-scoped resource** you depend on (a regional endpoint, a regional bucket, a regional queue) which is a single point of failure that no amount of multi-AZ fixes. That residual --- correlated regional failure --- is the entire thing multi-region buys, and it is worth being precise that it is a *small* residual on top of a *large* win from multi-AZ.
Follow: You said AZs are close enough for low latency. Close enough for what, exactly --- synchronous replication?
Yes, and that is the whole point of the distinction. AZ-to-AZ round trips are **single-digit milliseconds** (commonly ~1-2ms), so you can replicate **synchronously** across AZs and still return a fast write --- a synchronous multi-AZ commit adds a couple of milliseconds, which almost every system can absorb. That means multi-AZ gives you an **RPO of essentially zero** nearly for free: the standby in another AZ has every acknowledged write, so an AZ failure loses nothing. Cross-**region** synchronous replication would add ~100ms to every write, which is why you are pushed to **async** and immediately inherit a non-zero RPO. So the AZ/region distinction is not only blast radius --- it is the **latency budget that decides whether synchronous replication is affordable at all**. Multi-AZ: sync, RPO ~0, trivial. Multi-region: async, RPO > 0, and every hard problem in this topic follows from that single fact.
Senior: Knowing that the AZ/region gap is a **latency budget** --- sync replication is affordable across AZs (RPO ~0) and unaffordable across regions (RPO > 0) --- rather than just a bigger circle on a diagram, is what turns a definition into a design consequence.
Speak: Draw the blast radii: **"multi-AZ survives a data-center failure --- cheap, standard, and it lets you replicate synchronously, so RPO is basically zero. Multi-region survives a whole-region failure, and it forces async, which is where the RPO and the split-brain come from."** Then say most systems only need the first.

### SDE2 | active-passive basics

What is an active-passive multi-region setup?

Active-passive (also called active-standby or primary-secondary) means **one region actively serves all traffic (the active/primary), while a second region sits on standby (passive/secondary), ready to take over if the active fails**. Under normal operation only the active region handles requests; the passive region is kept up to date (data is replicated to it) but does not serve users. If the active region goes down, you **fail over** --- shift traffic to the passive region and promote it to active. Its appeal is **simplicity**: there is exactly one region serving and (usually) one place writes happen, so you avoid the hard problem of concurrent writes/conflicts across regions --- the standby is just a replica you promote. Its costs are that the standby is **idle capacity** you pay for but do not use for serving (unless you at least serve reads from it), and there is a **failover delay** (detecting the failure, redirecting traffic, promoting the standby --- your RTO) plus a potential **data-loss window** (anything not yet replicated when the active died --- your RPO). Active-passive is the natural fit when the goal is **disaster recovery** (survive a region loss) rather than latency, because it is far simpler than active-active and DR does not require the standby to serve in normal times.

Follow: The standby is idle capacity you are paying for. Can you at least serve reads from it --- and what breaks if you do?
You can, and it is tempting, but the moment you do you have stopped having a "simple" standby. The standby is an **asynchronous replica**, so it is **stale by the replication lag**, and that produces two visible bugs: a user who writes in the primary and immediately reads from the standby can **fail to see their own write** (a read-your-writes violation --- the bug users report within minutes), and two successive reads that land on different replicas can appear to **go backwards in time** (a monotonic-reads violation). So you can only serve reads that genuinely tolerate staleness --- analytics, dashboards, cacheable content, anything already behind a cache TTL --- and you must **pin a user to the primary for a window after they write**, or carry a version token their read waits on. The honest framing: serving reads from the standby is the first step from active-passive toward read-local/write-global, and it comes with a consistency story you now owe. It is a good trade --- just do not pretend it is free.
Follow: The standby never serves traffic. How do you know it will actually work when you need it?
You do not --- and that unexercised-path problem is the core weakness of active-passive. The standby **rots silently**: replication breaks and nobody notices, the standby is under-provisioned for real load, a config value or secret or third-party allowlist exists only in the primary, IAM and DNS are subtly wrong, the promotion script has never run in anger, and capacity you assumed would be there is not. None of this surfaces until the one moment you need it. The mitigations are all about **turning the unexercised into the exercised**: alarm on **replication lag** (lag above your RPO is an active incident, not a graph), build both regions from the **same infrastructure-as-code** so drift cannot accumulate, route **some real traffic** through the standby (even a small percentage of reads) so it is never cold and dark, and run scheduled **game days** that actually fail over and measure the real RTO and RPO. This is precisely the strongest argument *for* active-active: when both regions always serve, there is no untested path to rot.
Senior: Recognizing that the standby's real cost is not the idle bill but that it is an **unexercised path that rots silently** --- and naming lag alarms, shared IaC, and game days as the fix --- is the operational instinct that separates someone who has run a DR plan from someone who has drawn one.
Speak: Give the shape and then the catch: **"one region serves, a replicated standby waits, and on failure you shift traffic and promote it --- simple, one writer, no conflicts."** Then name the weakness before they do: the standby is unexercised, so it rots --- alarm on lag and prove it with game days.

### SDE2 | active-active basics

What is an active-active multi-region setup, and what does it add over active-passive?

Active-active means **multiple regions serve traffic simultaneously** --- each region actively handles requests (typically from nearby users), and all regions are "live." Over active-passive it adds two big things: **low latency everywhere** (each user is served from a nearby region rather than a single distant one, so a global user base gets good latency) and **no idle capacity** (every region does useful work, and losing one region just shifts its load to the others rather than activating a cold standby --- so failover can be faster and capacity is not wasted). The price is the **hardest problem in multi-region: concurrent writes**. Because multiple regions accept writes at once, the same data can be modified in two regions "simultaneously" (given cross-region replication lag), creating **conflicts** that must be resolved, and you cannot have cheap strong global consistency (coordinating every write across regions would add the cross-region latency you went active-active to avoid). So active-active buys latency and capacity efficiency at the cost of a real distributed-data problem --- conflict resolution, per-region data ownership, and eventual (not strong) cross-region consistency. It is the right choice when **low global latency** is the goal and you are willing to engineer the write/conflict story; if you only need DR, active-passive is much simpler.

Follow: You said concurrent writes create conflicts. Do they always --- or can you run active-active and never have one?
You can run active-active and never have a conflict, and that is what most systems calling themselves active-active actually do. A conflict requires **two regions writing the same datum concurrently**. If you partition write **ownership** so each datum has exactly one owning region --- route a user's writes to their home region --- then every region serves, but no two ever write the same row, so conflicts are **structurally impossible** and there is nothing to reconcile. That is read-local/write-global. You only get true conflicts when you deliberately allow **multi-master writes to the same key** (DynamoDB Global Tables, a multi-master Cassandra keyspace), and then you owe a resolution strategy --- last-write-wins, CRDTs, or sibling/merge. So "active-active" names two very different systems: active-active **serving** with single-writer ownership (common, safe, and what you almost always want), and active-active **writing** to the same data (rare, genuinely hard). Saying which one you mean is the whole answer.
Follow: You said losing a region just shifts load to the others. Is your remaining capacity actually enough?
Only if you provisioned for it --- and this is the part people skip. With N regions each running at some utilization, losing one dumps its traffic onto the survivors: two regions each at 60% utilization means the survivor must absorb 120%, and it falls over --- your "graceful" failover becomes a cascading outage. So active-active needs enough **headroom that N-1 regions can carry 100% of traffic**: at two regions, each runs at **50% or less** (which means you are paying for 2x capacity --- the same bill as an idle standby, just spread out and serving); at three regions each can run at ~66%, so the marginal cost of resilience *falls* as you add regions. The alternative is leaning on **autoscaling** to absorb the shift, but scaling takes minutes exactly when you are already degraded, the capacity may not be available (everyone's DR is scaling at the same moment), and the newly-scaled fleet has **cold caches**, so it hammers the database it is trying to protect. So "no idle capacity" is only true *relative to a dark standby*: the honest version is that the spare capacity is spread across regions and doing useful work instead of sitting idle --- but you still buy N/(N-1) of it.
Senior: Distinguishing **active-active serving (single-writer ownership, no conflicts) from active-active writing (multi-master, real conflicts)** --- and knowing that N-1 capacity headroom is what makes the "graceful" failure actually graceful --- is what separates a real active-active design from the diagram.
Speak: Say what it buys and what it costs in one breath: **"all regions serve --- low latency, no dark standby --- but concurrent writes to the same datum create conflicts."** Then take the escape hatch: give each datum one owning region and there are no conflicts to resolve; and size for N-1, or losing a region takes down the survivors.

### SDE2 | RTO and RPO

What are RTO and RPO, and why are they the core disaster-recovery metrics?

They are the two numbers that define your disaster-recovery requirement, and every DR design is really a way to hit a target RTO and RPO. **RTO --- Recovery Time Objective** --- is *how quickly you must be back up* after a disaster: the maximum acceptable downtime (e.g. "we must recover within 15 minutes"). It is driven by failover speed --- detecting the failure, shifting traffic, promoting the standby, warming capacity. **RPO --- Recovery Point Objective** --- is *how much data you can afford to lose*: the maximum acceptable data-loss window, expressed as time (e.g. "we can lose at most the last 5 seconds of writes"). It is driven by replication --- with asynchronous cross-region replication, anything written but not yet replicated when the region died is lost, so your RPO is roughly your replication lag; with synchronous replication RPO can be near zero (but at a latency cost). They are the core metrics because they turn "we need DR" into concrete, testable targets that dictate the architecture: a tight RTO demands a warm/hot standby and automated failover (a cold standby you rebuild from backups cannot recover in minutes); a tight RPO demands synchronous or very-low-lag replication (async replication with minutes of lag means minutes of data loss). Stating RTO and RPO explicitly --- and choosing the standby tier and replication mode to meet them --- is what separates a real DR plan from "we have a backup somewhere."

Follow: Your RPO is 5 seconds and your replication lag is normally 1 second. Are you meeting your RPO?
On average, yes --- which is not what an RPO is. An RPO is a bound on the **worst case at the moment of failure**, and replication lag is not a constant: it **spikes exactly when you are most likely to fail over**. A load surge, a network event, a bulk backfill, a schema migration, a slow or under-provisioned replica --- all of these push lag from a second to minutes, and several of them are precisely the conditions that precede a regional incident. So the number that matters is not the typical lag but the **p99 and the maximum**, and specifically the lag *during* the events that lead to failover. To actually meet a 5-second RPO you must **measure lag continuously and alarm when it exceeds the RPO**, and treat sustained breach as an incident --- because at that moment you are out of compliance with your own DR promise and nobody knows. A great many teams have an RPO in a document and a lag graph nobody looks at, which means the RPO is a hope, not a guarantee.
Follow: Which is harder to buy down --- RTO or RPO --- and what does each one cost you?
They are bought with completely different currencies, which is why you should never quote them as one number. **RPO is bought with replication**: to shrink it you replicate more often, and ultimately synchronously --- and the cost lands on the **hot write path** (a synchronous cross-region write pays the full ~100ms round trip, on every write, forever) and on **availability** (a synchronous write must block or degrade when the remote region is unreachable). Driving RPO to zero taxes every user, all the time. **RTO is bought with standby warmth and automation**: to shrink it you keep more of the standby running (backup-restore -> pilot light -> warm -> hot) and you automate detection and promotion --- and the cost is **idle infrastructure** plus the **risk of a false automated failover**. Driving RTO down costs money and failover risk, but it does *not* tax the steady-state write path. So the general shape: **RPO costs latency on every write; RTO costs money and failover risk.** That is exactly why most systems land on a small non-zero RPO (seconds, async) and spend their budget on RTO (a warm standby with automated, fenced failover) --- you would rather pay once for capacity than forever on every write.
Senior: Knowing that **RPO is paid for in write latency and RTO in idle capacity and failover risk** --- and that an RPO is a bound on the *worst* lag, not the average, so it must be alarmed on --- is what turns two acronyms into an actual architecture.
Speak: Define them as commitments, not trivia: **"RTO is how fast I'm back; RPO is how much data I can lose."** Then price them: RPO is bought with replication and paid for in write latency; RTO is bought with standby warmth and paid for in idle capacity --- so I alarm on replication lag, because a lag above my RPO means my DR promise is already broken.

### SDE3 | active-passive vs active-active

Active-passive or active-active --- how do you actually choose, and is one just better?

Neither is universally better; they solve different problems, so you choose by the **motivation**. **Active-passive** is the right answer for **disaster recovery**: it is far simpler --- one region serves, one writer, so no cross-region write conflicts and a clean consistency story --- and DR does not need the standby to serve in normal times, so you pay for idle standby capacity, a failover delay (RTO), and a data-loss window (RPO), but nothing harder. **Active-active** is for **genuinely global low latency**: every user is served from a nearby region and no capacity sits idle, but multiple regions accepting writes creates cross-region conflicts and rules out cheap strong global consistency. So the clean mapping is DR -> active-passive, global latency -> active-active. And there is a middle that most "global latency" systems actually want: **read-local, write-global** --- active-active *serving* with single-writer *ownership*. You replicate everywhere so reads are local and fast, but route each datum's writes to one owning region, so you get the latency win with no conflicts to resolve. The senior instinct is to reach for that middle first and only escalate to true multi-master writes when the data genuinely cannot be single-owned.

Follow: You said active-active fails over faster than active-passive. Why, mechanically?
Because in active-active there is **almost nothing to promote** --- the surviving regions are already live, serving, and warm, so "failover" is mostly a **routing change** (stop sending traffic to the dead region; its share spreads to the survivors) rather than the active-passive sequence of detect -> promote a standby's replica to a writable primary -> warm up capacity that was idle -> then shift traffic. Active-passive's RTO includes that promotion and warm-up; active-active pays almost none of it, so its RTO can be seconds. The catch is that this is only true if the survivors have the **capacity headroom** to absorb the dead region's load --- N-1 provisioning. Without it, the "instant" failover just cascades the outage onto the survivors, and you have traded a clean region loss for a total one. So active-active's fast failover is real, but it is bought with spare capacity, not for free.
Follow: If read-local/write-global gives local reads with no conflicts, why would anyone accept true multi-master writes?
Only when the data has **no single natural owner** and must be writable from multiple regions concurrently --- and you are willing to pay for conflict resolution to get it. Single-writer ownership works beautifully when each datum has an obvious home (a user's profile owned by their home region), but some workloads do not partition cleanly: a globally-shared counter, a document edited collaboratively from everywhere, inventory decremented in every region. For those, routing all writes to one region either reintroduces the cross-region write latency you went multi-region to avoid, or creates a single global write bottleneck. So you accept multi-master and owe a **conflict strategy** --- CRDTs where the data model allows (lossless, deterministic convergence), or detect-and-merge with version vectors --- and you reserve it for the narrow slice that truly cannot be single-owned. The default stays single-writer per datum; multi-master is the exception you justify, not the baseline.
Senior: Knowing that **active-passive is for DR and active-active is for latency**, that read-local/write-global is the conflict-free middle most systems actually want, and that active-active's faster failover is real *only* with N-1 capacity headroom --- rather than treating active-active as strictly superior --- is the topology judgment a senior round checks.
Speak: Pick by the motivation: **"active-passive for DR --- simpler, one writer, no conflicts; active-active for global latency --- but I'd usually reach for read-local/write-global: replicate for local reads, route each datum's writes to one owning region, so there's nothing to reconcile."** Then note active-active only fails over fast if the survivors have N-1 headroom.

### SDE3 | async vs sync cross-region replication

When do you replicate synchronously across regions versus asynchronously, and what does each cost?

**Asynchronous replication** (the default across regions): a write is committed in its origin region and returned to the client immediately, and the change is shipped to other regions in the background. It costs you a **replication lag** --- other regions are behind by the network delay plus processing --- which becomes a **data-loss window (RPO)** if the origin region dies before replicating, and it allows **stale reads** and **write conflicts** in the lag window. Its benefit is **low write latency** (writes do not wait for a cross-region round trip), which is usually essential given ~100ms cross-region RTT. **Synchronous replication**: a write is not acknowledged until another region (or a quorum of regions) has durably received it. It gives you a **near-zero RPO** (no acknowledged write is lost, since it is confirmed elsewhere before returning) and stronger consistency, but it costs **the full cross-region latency on every write** (each write waits ~100ms+ for the remote ack) and **reduced availability** (if the remote region is unreachable, writes block or must fall back). The decision: use **synchronous** only for the small set of data where you cannot tolerate *any* loss and can accept the write-latency hit (critical financial transactions, and even then often within a region or a nearby region pair, or via a system like Spanner that engineers this with special infrastructure); use **asynchronous** for essentially everything else, and manage the resulting RPO and conflicts. Most multi-region systems are asynchronous with a well-understood RPO, because paying 100ms per write globally is rarely acceptable --- so you accept a small data-loss window instead.

Follow: You default to async and inherit an RPO. How do you make that RPO a real guarantee instead of a hope?
By **measuring replication lag continuously and alarming when it exceeds the RPO** --- because the RPO is a bound on the lag *at the instant of failure*, and lag is not constant: it spikes on exactly the events that precede an incident (a load surge, a bulk backfill, a slow replica, a network wobble, a schema migration). So "our RPO is 5 seconds" is only real if you know the lag is under 5 seconds *right now*, and a lag graph nobody watches is not a guarantee. You treat sustained lag above the RPO as an active incident --- you are out of compliance with your own DR promise --- and you verify the whole thing under game days that measure the *actual* RPO on a real failover, not the average lag on a calm afternoon. The number to put in front of the business is concrete: lag times write-rate is a count of writes at risk, so a "few seconds" RPO at 5,000 writes/sec is fifteen thousand transactions you could lose.
Follow: Is there a middle ground between full-sync-everywhere and full-async?
Yes, two of them, and both are common. **Semi-synchronous**: replicate synchronously to *one* nearby region (or a quorum) and asynchronously to the rest --- so an acknowledged write is guaranteed to exist in at least two places (near-zero RPO for that pair) without waiting on *every* region, and you eat only the nearest region's round trip, not the farthest. **Per-datum**: most data is async with a small RPO, while a narrow no-loss slice (a ledger, a payment) is synchronous and pays the full cross-region latency. The trap in semi-sync is the **silent downgrade** --- some implementations (MySQL semi-sync) fall back to asynchronous when the sync follower fails to ack within a timeout, so under the exact load spike where you most need the guarantee you quietly lose it, and an ack stops meaning "two copies." You must alarm on the *downgrade event itself*, not merely configure the timeout, or your guarantee evaporates precisely when it matters.
Senior: Turning the async/sync choice into a **number** --- RPO is roughly lag times write-rate, priced against the per-write cost of a synchronous round trip (~1-2ms cross-AZ, ~60-100ms cross-region) --- and knowing that semi-sync **silently downgrades to async on timeout** so you must alarm on the downgrade, is what separates a real answer from reciting the definitions.
Speak: Make it quantitative: **"async keeps writes fast but the lag is my RPO --- lag times write-rate is the data I'd lose, so at 3 seconds and 5,000 writes a second that's fifteen thousand writes. Sync gives near-zero RPO but pays the full ~100ms cross-region round trip on every write."** Then land on semi-sync as the middle, and name its trap: it downgrades to async on timeout, so alarm on the downgrade.

### SDE3 | failover mechanics

Walk through the mechanics of an automated regional failover in an active-passive setup.

Detect, redirect, promote --- with a safeguard at each step. **Detect**: a health-checking system, ideally external to both regions (a global routing service like Route 53 health checks, or a global load balancer), probes the active region from multiple vantage points; failover triggers only when it is convincingly down --- multiple failed checks over a short window, so you do not react to a transient blip or to a network hiccup between the checker and the region. **Redirect**: shift traffic to the secondary, typically by **DNS failover** (update the health-checked routing record so the hostname resolves to the secondary --- subject to DNS TTL, so you keep TTLs low for DR-critical records) or via a **global load balancer / anycast** that stops routing to the failed region. **Promote**: make the secondary able to serve writes --- **promote its database replica to primary** (the async replica becomes the new writable primary), scale up any capacity that was not fully warm, and flip application config so the secondary knows it is now active. Then **verify** the secondary is genuinely serving correctly, and later **fail back** carefully (re-establish the original as a replica of the new primary, sync it, and switch back during a controlled window --- avoiding a second outage and a fresh split-brain). The two critical safeguards: **reliable detection** (a false failover is an outage you cause yourself, and DNS/promotion are slow to reverse) and **fencing / single-writer guarantees** so that if the "failed" region is actually still alive, you do not end up with two primaries taking writes.

Follow: Detection says the region is down. How do you avoid failing over on a blip that would have self-healed?
By making the trigger **conservative and multi-signal**, because the cost of a false failover is comparable to the outage you are protecting against. Concretely: require **multiple consecutive failed health checks over a short window** (not a single miss), probe from **multiple independent vantage points** (so a network problem between one checker and the region does not look like a region failure), and check a **meaningful signal** --- a real request path succeeding, not just a TCP ping to a load balancer that is up while the app behind it is dead. You tune the threshold to trade detection speed against false-positive rate: too twitchy and you flap (failing back and forth on noise); too slow and your RTO suffers. For the highest-stakes systems you keep a **human approval gate** (automation detects and *prepares* the failover, a human confirms it is really down and hits go), precisely because a machine failing over on ambiguous evidence can turn a five-minute blip into a split-brain incident. The instinct being tested is that "detect the failure" is itself a hard problem, not a given.
Follow: You promote the standby's async replica to primary. What did you just lose, and what has to be true for it to serve correctly?
You lost **whatever had not yet replicated** when the primary died --- the writes in the replication lag window, which is your RPO made concrete (lag times write-rate). Those transactions are simply gone from the new primary; if they were financial, you now have a reconciliation problem, which is why a no-loss slice needs sync replication. For the promoted replica to serve *correctly*, three things must hold: it must have **fully applied its replication backlog** (an async replica may have received events into its log but not yet replayed them, so promoting before it drains means serving stale or inconsistent data), the application must be **reconfigured to treat it as writable** (a read replica often rejects writes until promoted), and the old primary must be **fenced** so it cannot come back and also accept writes. Skip the drain and you serve a stale view; skip the fence and you get split-brain. So promotion is not a flip of a switch --- it is "drain the backlog, flip to writable, fence the old one," and the data you lose is bounded by the RPO you signed up for.
Senior: Treating **detection as a hard problem** (conservative, multi-signal, often human-gated because a false failover is as costly as the outage) and knowing that **promotion means drain-the-backlog, flip-to-writable, and fence-the-old-primary** --- with the lost data bounded by the RPO --- is the operational depth that separates someone who has run a failover from someone who has read the diagram.
Speak: Give the three beats with the safeguards: **"detect convincingly --- multiple checks from multiple vantage points, because a false failover is an outage I caused; redirect at a global routing layer; promote the standby --- drain its replication backlog, flip it to writable, and fence the old primary so it can't come back as a second writer."** Then note the data I lose is exactly my RPO.

### SDE3 | data residency and sovereignty

How does data residency change a multi-region design?

Data residency (data-sovereignty) requirements --- laws like GDPR or national rules that mandate certain data physically stay within a specific country or region --- turn multi-region from an availability/latency choice into a **data-placement constraint**, and it reshapes the design around *keeping data in the right place* rather than replicating it everywhere. Concretely: you can no longer freely replicate all data to all regions (which DR and active-active normally want), because, say, EU users' personal data must remain in the EU and must *not* be copied to the US. So you **partition data by residency** --- shard users by their home region and keep their regulated data only in that region's store (an EU user's data lives in and stays in the EU). This interacts with topology: an active-active system must ensure a write for an EU user lands in and stays in the EU region (data ownership aligned to residency), and cross-region replication becomes **selective** (replicate what you may, keep what you must local). It also complicates DR --- you cannot fail an EU region's regulated data over to a US region, so the standby for EU-resident data must itself be in-jurisdiction (another EU region) --- and complicates global features, which may need to query per region rather than a single replicated store, or store only non-regulated fields globally. The senior point is that residency is a **hard constraint that dictates where data can live**, so you make **region the unit of data ownership by jurisdiction**: pin regulated data to compliant regions, replicate only within-jurisdiction for DR, and keep the global system working by partitioning users by home region rather than replicating everything everywhere.

Follow: A user is an EU resident but travels to the US and uses the app there. Where do their writes go, and what actually crosses the border?
Their writes still go to their **home (EU) region** --- residency follows the *data subject*, not the request origin, so an EU resident's regulated data stays EU-owned wherever they physically are. What happens is a **cross-region hop**: the US region their request lands on forwards their writes to the EU owner (adding latency for that roaming user, which is acceptable because it is the exception, not the norm), and reads of their regulated data either come from the EU or are simply slower while abroad. What must *not* cross the border is the **regulated payload** being persisted in the US --- so you are careful that the US edge terminates the connection and proxies to the EU rather than caching or storing personal data locally. You can still serve non-regulated, non-personal data (a product catalog) from the US region normally. The discipline is that the *routing* can be global but the *storage* of regulated data is pinned; a roaming user pays latency, not a compliance violation.
Follow: You need a global view --- say a support tool or analytics --- that spans EU and US users. How do you build it without copying regulated data out?
Two standard moves, used together. **Query per-region and aggregate**: the global tool fans a query out to each region's store, each region returns results computed *locally* (in-jurisdiction), and you aggregate the answers rather than copying the raw personal data into one place --- a scatter-gather that keeps regulated data home. **Store only non-regulated fields globally**: you replicate a minimal, non-personal projection everywhere (a user id and their home region, an anonymized/pseudonymized key, aggregate counts) so global features can function on the non-regulated slice, and drill into personal fields only by going to the owning region. For analytics specifically, you often compute per-region aggregates in-jurisdiction and ship only the *aggregates* (which are not personal data) to a global warehouse. The principle is that a "global view" does not require a global *copy*: you either bring the computation to the data (per-region query) or replicate only the fields the law lets you, so the regulated data never leaves its jurisdiction even though the feature spans jurisdictions.
Senior: Recognizing that residency follows the **data subject** (so a roaming user's writes hop back to their home region rather than persisting locally), and that a global view is built by **querying per-region and aggregating** or replicating only a non-regulated projection --- never by copying regulated data out --- is the compliance-aware design that most candidates miss entirely.
Speak: Frame residency as placement, not features: **"residency makes region the unit of data ownership by law --- an EU resident's regulated data lives in and stays in the EU, even when they travel, so a roaming write hops back to the home region. I replicate only within-jurisdiction, including for DR, and build global views by querying per-region and aggregating rather than copying personal data out."**

### SDE3 | read-local, write-global

Explain the read-local, write-global pattern and why it is popular.

Read-local/write-global (a single-writer-with-local-read-replicas model) is a middle ground that captures most of active-active's benefit while sidestepping its hardest problem. The idea: **replicate data to all regions so reads are served locally** (low latency, from a nearby replica), **but route all writes for a given piece of data to a single owning region** --- the "home" region for that datum or user, which is the sole writer. Reads --- usually the large majority of traffic --- get local latency from the regional replica; writes go (cross-region if needed) to the one region that owns the data, which then replicates the change out to the others. It is popular because it **eliminates write conflicts** (exactly one writer per datum, so no two regions can concurrently modify the same data --- no conflict resolution needed) while still giving **local read performance everywhere**, which is what most read-heavy applications actually need. The cost is that **writes pay cross-region latency when the user is not in their data's home region**, and there is still async **replication lag** for propagation to other regions (so a user might read a slightly stale value from their local replica right after a write elsewhere --- addressed with read-your-writes techniques). This pattern is the pragmatic default for "multi-region for latency" precisely because it decouples the two concerns: reads scale and localize trivially via replicas, and writes stay simple and conflict-free via single-region ownership --- you only pay the cross-region cost on writes, and only when the writer is remote from the data's home.

Follow: How do you decide which region owns a given datum, and what happens when that guess is wrong for most of a user's traffic?
You pick the owner by **where the datum is most written** --- usually the user's home region, inferred from signup location, declared region, or observed access pattern --- because writes are the only thing that pays the cross-region tax, so you want the owner near the writer. When the guess is wrong --- a user who signed up in the US but now lives in and writes from the EU --- *every* write pays a US round trip, which is the exact latency you went multi-region to avoid, so ownership must be **movable**. You migrate the datum's home region: quiesce writes briefly (or route them through), copy/confirm the data is current in the new region, flip the ownership pointer, and let the old region become a read replica. This is a controlled, rare operation, not per-request, so you do it on a signal (a sustained pattern of remote writes) rather than thrashing. The senior point is that single-writer ownership is only conflict-free *and* fast if the owner tracks the writer, so you need a deliberate, safe **ownership-migration** path, not a static home decided once at signup.
Follow: Reads are local, but a user just wrote to their remote home region and then reads their local (lagging) replica. They do not see their own write. How do you fix that specifically?
That is a **read-your-writes** violation caused by the local replica lagging the home region, and you fix it without making all reads global. The cheapest fix: for a **short window after a write, pin that user's reads to the home region** (or route them through it), so they read the fresh copy while their local replica catches up --- a per-user, per-window redirect, not a global change. The more precise fix: the write returns a **version token / log sequence number (LSN)**, and the user's subsequent reads carry it; the local replica **waits until it has applied at least that version** before answering (or the read is routed to a replica known to be at least that fresh). That gives read-your-writes with mostly-local reads --- you only pay the cross-region cost during the brief catch-up window, and only for the user who just wrote. The general principle: you owe read-your-writes to the *writer*, so you tighten consistency for *them* specifically (pin-after-write or a version watermark) rather than paying strong consistency on every read for everyone.
Senior: Knowing that single-writer ownership is only conflict-free *and* fast if the owner **tracks the writer** (so you need a safe ownership-migration path, not a static home) and that read-your-writes is repaired with a **pin-after-write window or a version watermark** rather than global reads, is what turns the pattern from a slogan into a working design.
Speak: Give the pattern and its one bug: **"read-local, write-global --- replicate everywhere for local reads, but each datum has one owning region as its sole writer, so there are zero write conflicts. I only pay cross-region latency on writes, and only when the writer is away from the data's home."** Then pre-empt the follow: the one bug is a stale local read after a remote write, fixed by pinning the writer to the home region for a short window or waiting on a version token.

### SDE3 | warm, cold, and pilot-light standby

What are the standby readiness tiers for DR, and how do they trade cost against RTO?

They are points on a spectrum from "cheap but slow to recover" to "expensive but instant," and you pick the cheapest one that meets your RTO/RPO. **Backup-and-restore (cold)**: no running standby --- you keep backups (and ideally infrastructure-as-code), and on disaster you provision a new environment and restore from backup. Cheapest (you run nothing extra), but the slowest RTO (hours --- spin up infra, restore data) and worst RPO (as stale as your last backup). **Pilot light**: a **minimal always-on core** in the standby region --- typically the data layer replicating continuously (so data is current, good RPO) with application/compute scaled to near-zero, ready to scale up on failover. Cheaper than a full standby (you pay mainly for replicated storage and a small footprint), with a moderate RTO (scale up compute, faster than building from scratch). **Warm standby**: a **fully functional but scaled-down** copy always running in the standby region (all components live, just smaller), so on failover you scale up and redirect --- faster RTO (minutes, everything is already running) at higher cost (a whole second environment, albeit small). **Hot standby / active-active**: a **full-scale** second region --- near-instant failover (little to promote, capacity already there) at the highest cost (a full duplicate, or genuinely active-active). The trade is monotonic: more always-on capacity in the standby -> faster recovery -> more cost. Most systems that need real DR land at **pilot-light or warm standby** (current data via replication, fast-ish recovery via pre-provisioned or quickly-scaled compute), reserving hot/active-active for the lowest-RTO, highest-value systems.

Follow: Notice the tiers vary compute warmth but they all keep data replicating. Why is the data layer the part you never scale to zero?
Because **RTO you can buy back quickly, but RPO you cannot buy back at all**. Compute is fast to summon --- pilot-light scales up in minutes, and even a cold restore eventually stands the app back up --- so a colder compute tier costs you *recovery time*, which is recoverable. But if the data layer is not continuously replicating, the only copy in the standby is your last **backup**, so your RPO collapses to "everything since the last snapshot" --- potentially hours of lost writes that are simply *gone* and cannot be reconstructed after the region is gone. That asymmetry is why every tier above pure backup-restore keeps the **data replicating live** even while compute sleeps: the data is the irreplaceable part, so you pay to keep it current, and you economize on compute (which you can conjure) rather than on replication (which you cannot). Put differently: the tiers are really about how warm the *compute* is; the *data* stays hot in all of them because a cold data layer means a bad RPO you can never recover.
Follow: Pilot-light scales compute up on failover. What is the risk in that scale-up, exactly when you are already in an incident?
That the scale-up **fails or is too slow at the worst possible moment** --- and you only find out during the disaster. The concrete risks: the capacity you assumed would be available **is not** (a regional event may have others scaling into the same pool, or you hit an account/service quota), the newly-launched fleet comes up with **cold caches** and hammers the database it is supposed to protect (a thundering-herd on a system that is already fragile), config or secrets or dependencies that lived only in the primary are **missing** in the scaled-up standby, and the scale-up path itself has simply **never run under real load**, so a bug in it surfaces now. The mitigations are exactly the DR-testing discipline: run **game days** that actually scale up and fail over so the path is exercised, pre-warm or reserve capacity for the critical tier so you are not depending on an on-demand pool during a regional event, build both regions from the **same IaC** so nothing is missing, and be honest that pilot-light's RTO includes real scale-up risk --- if you cannot tolerate it, you pay for warm standby instead. The point is that "scale up on failover" is a promise you must prove, not assume.
Senior: Understanding that the tiers really vary **compute warmth while data stays hot in all of them** --- because RPO is unrecoverable and RTO is buyable --- and that pilot-light's scale-up is a real, must-be-tested risk (unavailable capacity, cold caches, missing config) rather than a guaranteed step, is the DR-economics judgment a senior round rewards.
Speak: Lay out the spectrum by cost vs RTO: **"cold backup-restore is cheapest and slowest; pilot-light keeps data replicating but compute near-zero; warm standby runs a scaled-down full copy; hot/active-active is a full duplicate. I pick the cheapest tier that hits my RTO --- usually pilot-light or warm."** Then the insight: data stays hot in every tier because RPO is unrecoverable, and I test the scale-up because that is where pilot-light bites.

### SDE3 | testing disaster recovery

Why is testing failover essential, and what does a good DR test look like?

Because **an untested DR plan is not a DR plan --- it is an assumption**, and multi-region failover is exactly the kind of complex, rarely-exercised procedure that quietly rots and fails when you finally need it. The failure modes hide until tested: the standby's data replication silently broke weeks ago (so failover reveals a huge RPO or missing data); DNS TTLs are too high so traffic does not actually shift within your RTO; the standby was under-provisioned and falls over under real load; a config or secret or dependency exists only in the primary; promotion scripts have bugs; or nobody actually knows the runbook. You only discover these by **exercising failover for real**. A good DR test is a **game day**: on a schedule, deliberately fail over to the secondary region (in production or a production-like environment) --- simulate the primary going down, execute the *actual* failover procedure, and **measure the real RTO and RPO** against your targets, verifying the secondary genuinely serves correct traffic under representative load. Good practice includes testing regularly (so it stays current as the system evolves), testing the *full* path (detection, traffic shift, promotion, and failback), verifying data integrity after (no loss beyond RPO, no corruption), and ideally continuous validation --- some organizations run active-active partly *because* both regions are always exercised, or use chaos engineering to routinely kill a region. The senior stance: **you do not have DR until you have failed over under test and hit your RTO/RPO** --- the plan on paper is worthless until proven, and the first real failover should never be during an actual disaster.

Follow: Failing over in production sounds terrifying. How do you run a real game day without risking a real outage?
By **de-risking the blast radius while keeping the test real**, in graduated steps. You start in a **production-like staging** environment to shake out the obvious bugs cheaply, then move toward production because staging never fully reproduces prod's data volume, config drift, and dependencies. In production you **announce the window** (so an on-call team is watching and it is not confused with a real incident), you have a **fast rollback** rehearsed (fail *back* quickly if the secondary misbehaves), and you can **scope** the test --- fail over a fraction of traffic, or one service, or a non-peak window --- before doing a full cutover. The point is that a game day is a *controlled* failover with a human ready to abort, which is categorically safer than the *uncontrolled* failover you will otherwise experience during a real disaster with no rehearsal. The teams that do this well treat the small risk of a game-day hiccup as the price of *not* discovering their DR is broken during an actual region loss --- and the more you run them, the lower that risk gets, because the path stops being novel.
Follow: The game day passed six months ago. Why can you not just trust that result?
Because **DR rots continuously as the system changes around it**, so a six-month-old pass certifies a system that no longer exists. Since then you have added services, changed configs, rotated secrets, altered dependencies, grown data volume, and modified the very failover scripts --- any of which can silently break the standby: a new service deployed only to the primary, a secret added but not replicated, a schema change the replica cannot apply, a capacity assumption invalidated by growth. Replication lag may have crept above the RPO. The runbook may reference a step that changed. This is why DR testing has to be **regular and automated**, not a one-time certification --- you re-run game days on a cadence (and ideally continuously validate, e.g. always-on active-active or scheduled chaos) so the plan is verified against the *current* system. The stance is that DR is a **freshness-sensitive guarantee**: it is only as good as its last successful, representative test, and a passing test decays the moment you deploy the next change.
Senior: Reframing DR as a **freshness-sensitive guarantee that rots as the system changes** --- so it demands regular, automated game days measuring real RTO/RPO, run as controlled failovers with rehearsed rollback rather than a one-time certification --- is the operational maturity that separates a real DR program from a runbook in a wiki.
Speak: State it as a rule: **"an untested DR plan is an assumption. I run scheduled game days that actually fail over and measure real RTO and RPO against the targets --- controlled, with rollback rehearsed --- and I re-run them regularly, because DR rots as the system changes. The first real failover should never be during an actual disaster."**

### SDE3 | read-your-writes across regions

A user updates their profile, and their next request lands on a different region's replica showing the old value. Walk me through giving them read-your-writes across regions.

This is the canonical cross-region consistency bug, and it comes straight from the physics: with async replication, a write applied in one region has not yet propagated to the replica the user's next read hits, so they see stale data --- and it is most jarring when it is *their own* write that vanished. The framing is that you owe **read-your-writes** (a user always sees their own writes) to the writer specifically, and you provide it without paying strong consistency on every read for everyone. The two standard mechanisms: **pin-after-write** --- for a short window after a user writes, route their reads to the region that took the write (the home/owning region for their data), so they read the fresh copy while other replicas catch up; and the **version watermark** --- the write returns a version token or log sequence number (LSN), the client carries it on subsequent reads, and a replica only answers once it has applied at least that version (otherwise it waits, or the read is routed to a replica known to be that fresh). Pin-after-write is simpler and coarse (all of that user's reads go home briefly); the watermark is precise (only reads that depend on the write wait, and only until the replica is caught up). Either way, the cost is bounded --- you tighten consistency for the one user who just wrote, during the brief replication-lag window, rather than making all reads global.

Follow: Pin-after-write sends the user's reads to their home region for a while. What is the failure mode of that approach?
Two, and both matter. First, **it does not survive a home-region failure or a re-route**: if the home region goes down (or the user's session is load-balanced elsewhere) during the pin window, their reads move to a replica that may be *behind* the write, so they travel backwards in time exactly when the system is already degraded --- the pin is a heuristic, not a guarantee. Second, **it costs the writer their local-read latency**: for the pin window, that user's reads pay the cross-region trip to the home region instead of being served locally, so a chatty client right after a write feels slow. The mitigations push you toward the **version watermark**, which is the real guarantee: instead of trusting stickiness, you carry the LSN and *choose or wait for* a replica that has applied it, so a re-route just picks a fresh-enough replica rather than a stale one. So pin-after-write is the cheap 80% answer; when you need it to actually hold under failover and re-routing, the watermark is what makes it correct, and stickiness becomes just an optimization that usually makes the wait unnecessary.
Follow: There is a subtler anomaly than read-your-writes here --- two users, where one reads a value that causally depends on another's write. What is it?
That is the **consistent-prefix / causal-ordering** anomaly, and it is the one candidates forget. Reads can reflect writes **out of causal order**: user A posts a question, user B (who saw it) posts an answer, and a third observer's reads --- served from replicas at different lag --- show the *answer before the question*. It arises when causally-related writes live on **different partitions or in different regions that replicate at different rates**, each with its own ordering and no global order across them, so a slower stream can deliver the earlier write later. Note that single-writer-per-datum does *not* fix this, because the problem is *between* independent writes, not concurrent writes to one datum. The fixes: keep causally-related writes in the **same partition/region** so one order governs them, or track the causal dependency explicitly --- attach a "happens-after" version token so the read of the answer waits for a replica that has also seen the question. So across regions you owe not just read-your-writes (a user sees *their own* writes) but, where it matters, **causal consistency** (a read never shows an effect before its cause), and you buy the latter only where the causal link is user-visible.
Senior: Providing read-your-writes with a **version watermark** (not just sticky pinning, which silently voids on re-route or region failure) and recognizing the distinct **consistent-prefix / causal-ordering** anomaly --- correctly attributing it to causally-related writes across independently-ordered regions, which single-writer-per-datum does not fix --- is genuine distributed-systems literacy most candidates never reach.
Speak: Name the guarantee and the two mechanisms: **"they didn't see their own write because the local replica lags the region that took it. I give read-your-writes by pinning that user to the home region for a short window, or precisely by returning a version token on the write and having reads wait for a replica that's applied it."** Then flag the deeper one: consistent-prefix, where a read shows an effect before its cause, needs causal tracking, not just single-writer ownership.

### SDE3 | bringing a new region online

You are adding a third region to a running active-active system. Walk me through bringing it online without downtime.

The core challenge is that a new region starts **empty**, so you cannot just point traffic at it --- you have to get its data current, verify it, and *then* let it serve, in that order, without disturbing the two live regions. The sequence: (1) **Stand up the infrastructure** from the same infrastructure-as-code as the existing regions (so it is a true peer, not a hand-built snowflake with drift), but keep it out of the traffic-routing pool. (2) **Backfill the data** --- take a consistent snapshot from an existing region and restore it into the new region, then start replication so the new region **catches up** the delta of writes that happened during the snapshot/restore; you watch the replication lag converge toward zero. (3) **Verify** the new region is genuinely consistent and caught up --- lag under threshold, spot-check data integrity, run health checks against real request paths --- before it takes any traffic. (4) **Add it as a read replica first**: route a *small* fraction of local read traffic to it, ramping up while watching latency and correctness, so any problem shows on a slice, not everyone. (5) **Grant it write ownership last**: for active-active, decide which data it will *own* (e.g. migrate the users whose home should now be this region) and cut those over deliberately, so you are not introducing a new writer for data mid-flight. Throughout, the two existing regions keep serving normally --- the new region is dark until proven, then reads, then writes --- so there is no downtime, just a controlled ramp.

Follow: During the backfill, writes keep flowing in the two live regions. How do you catch the new region up to a moving target without a stop-the-world?
With the standard **snapshot-plus-catch-up-log** technique, which is exactly how you add a replica to a running database. You take a **consistent snapshot** at a known replication position (an LSN/timestamp), restore that snapshot into the new region --- which takes a while, during which more writes accumulate --- and then **start streaming the replication log from that recorded position forward**, so the new region replays every write that happened since the snapshot. Because the write throughput is finite and the replay is fast, the new region's lag **shrinks over time** and converges: it is catching up faster than new writes arrive, so it closes the gap to a small steady-state lag. You never stop the world --- the live regions keep writing, the new region keeps replaying from the log, and you simply *wait* until lag is under your threshold before promoting it to serve. The key is capturing the exact log position of the snapshot so there is **no gap and no overlap** between the snapshot and the streamed delta; get that boundary wrong and the new region is silently missing or double-applying writes.
Follow: You are done and want to remove one of the *old* regions instead. What is different about draining a region versus adding one?
Draining is the mirror image, and the ordering flips: you **stop it owning writes first**, then **stop it serving reads**, then **decommission** --- the reverse of add's reads-then-writes ramp. Concretely: (1) **migrate away its write ownership** --- every datum this region is the home for must be re-homed to another region (quiesce those writes briefly, confirm the new owner is current, flip ownership), because tearing down a region that is still the sole writer for some data would orphan those writes. (2) **drain its read traffic** --- shift its share to the other regions gradually (respecting DNS TTL / anycast so clients actually move), watching that the survivors have the **capacity** to absorb it (the N-1 headroom question). (3) **confirm nothing depends on it** --- no data only-owned-here, no traffic still routed here, no other region replicating *from* it as its source --- and then decommission. The subtle risks are leaving a datum still owned by the dying region (data loss) and dumping its traffic onto survivors that cannot take it (a self-inflicted overload). So adding is "data first, then reads, then writes"; draining is "writes off, then reads off, then tear down" --- and both are controlled ramps with a verification gate, never a flip.
Senior: Knowing the **ordering discipline** --- add a region as data-then-reads-then-writes and drain it as writes-then-reads-then-teardown --- and the **snapshot-at-a-known-log-position plus catch-up** technique for backfilling a live system without stop-the-world (with the boundary-capture as the subtle correctness point), is the operational depth that shows you have actually run a multi-region fleet, not just designed one.
Speak: Give the ordered ramp: **"a new region starts empty, so: stand it up from the same IaC, backfill from a snapshot at a known log position and stream the delta until lag converges, verify it's caught up, then add it as a read replica on a small traffic slice, and grant it write ownership last."** Then the mirror: draining reverses it --- move write ownership off first, then drain reads (checking the survivors have N-1 capacity), then tear down.

### Staff | split-brain and preventing it

What is split-brain in a multi-region context, and how do you prevent it?

Split-brain is when a **network partition** cuts the regions off from each other, but **both regions continue accepting writes as if they are in charge** --- so each independently modifies data, and when the partition heals the two histories have **diverged irreconcilably** (conflicting writes to the same data, both "committed," with no single truth to reconcile to). It is the most dangerous multi-region failure because it silently corrupts data and is easy to cause accidentally during failover: if the primary region is not actually dead but merely *unreachable* from the failover controller, and you promote the secondary to primary, you now have **two primaries taking writes** --- classic split-brain. Prevention is about ensuring **at most one region can write a given datum at any time**, via one of three mechanisms: **single-writer per datum** (only the owning region ever writes it --- read-local/write-global inherently avoids split-brain because the other regions never accept writes for that data even if they cannot see the owner); **quorum / consensus** (writes require a majority of regions/nodes to agree, so a minority partition *cannot* form a quorum and therefore cannot accept writes --- exactly how Raft/Paxos-based systems avoid split-brain, at the cost of needing an odd number of regions/witnesses and blocking the minority side); and **fencing** (when you promote a new primary, you *fence off* the old one --- revoke its ability to write via a fencing token, lease, or STONITH-style isolation --- so that if it was only unreachable, it cannot resume as a second writer). The staff framing: split-brain is prevented not by *hoping* the old primary is dead but by **structurally guaranteeing a single writer** through ownership, quorum, or fencing --- and the classic mistake is automated failover that promotes a secondary without fencing the primary, turning a partition into permanent data divergence.

Follow: A fencing token revokes the old primary's write ability. But it is partitioned --- you cannot reach it to revoke anything. How does fencing actually work?
That is the crux, and the answer is that fencing does not require *reaching* the old primary --- it works at the **resource the writes land on**, not at the isolated node. The canonical mechanism (from Kleppmann's lock analysis): every write carries a **monotonically increasing fencing token**, and the shared resource --- the database, the storage layer, the log --- **rejects any write bearing a token older than the highest it has seen**. When you promote the new primary you issue it a *higher* token; the moment it writes, the resource's watermark advances, and the old primary --- even if it is alive and still thinks it is in charge --- gets its writes **rejected** because its token is now stale. So you never had to contact the partitioned node; you made its credentials expire at the resource. The lease variant is the same idea in time: the primary holds a **time-bounded lease** and must renew it, so a partitioned primary's lease simply *expires* and it stops being allowed to write, while the new primary takes a fresh lease only after the old one is guaranteed dead. Fencing is enforcement at the write target, which is why it works against a node you cannot even talk to.
Follow: Quorum stops the minority from writing --- but it means the minority partition is *down* for writes. Is that not just choosing unavailability?
Yes, and that is CAP made concrete: under a partition you **cannot have both** consistency (no split-brain) and availability (both sides writable) for the same data --- quorum deliberately **chooses consistency**, so the minority side rejects writes rather than diverging. That is the right choice when divergence is catastrophic (financial data, anything where you cannot merge two truths), because a brief write-outage on the minority is far cheaper than permanent corruption. But it is a *choice*, not a free lunch, and the alternative is equally legitimate for different data: an **AP** design (Dynamo-style) keeps *both* sides writable during the partition and accepts that it must **reconcile conflicts on heal** (via CRDTs or version-vector merge) --- available but eventually consistent. So the honest framing is that you pick per-data: **CP (quorum/single-writer, minority unavailable) where divergence is unacceptable; AP (both write, reconcile later) where availability matters more and the data can converge**. Split-brain is only a *bug* in a system that wanted consistency but failed to enforce a single writer --- an AP system that *plans* to merge is not split-brained, it is doing what it chose. Knowing which regime your data is in is the whole judgment.
Senior: Explaining that **fencing enforces at the write resource via a monotonic token or an expiring lease** (so it works against a node you cannot reach) and framing quorum's minority-unavailability as a deliberate **CP choice versus a legitimate AP alternative that reconciles on heal** --- rather than treating split-brain as a single bug with a single fix --- is the distributed-systems judgment that lands a Staff signal.
Speak: Define it as the failure you design against: **"split-brain is a partition where both regions keep writing and diverge irreconcilably --- the classic cause is promoting a secondary while the primary is only unreachable, not dead."** Then the three preventions: single-writer ownership, a quorum so the minority can't write, or fencing --- and fencing works by making the old primary's token stale at the storage layer, so you never have to reach it.

### Staff | multi-region writes and conflict resolution

In active-active with multi-region writes, how do you handle write conflicts?

You either **avoid** conflicts by design (strongly preferred) or **resolve** them with a defined strategy when you cannot. **Avoidance --- single-writer per datum**: partition ownership so each piece of data has exactly one region that may write it (route a user's/entity's writes to their home region), so concurrent conflicting writes are structurally impossible --- this is read-local/write-global, and it is the cleanest answer because there is simply nothing to reconcile. When you genuinely need multiple regions to write the same data, you need a **conflict-resolution strategy** and must accept eventual consistency: **Last-Write-Wins (LWW)** --- attach a timestamp/version and the latest write wins --- simple but it **loses data** (the discarded write silently vanishes) and depends on clock sync (dangerous across regions); acceptable only when losing one of two concurrent writes is tolerable. **Conflict-free replicated data types (CRDTs)** --- data structures mathematically designed so concurrent updates **merge deterministically without loss** (counters, sets, sequences) --- the strong choice when your data fits a CRDT (collaborative editing, counters, carts modeled as add/remove sets), because every region converges to the same value automatically. **Version vectors / application-level merge** --- detect the conflict (vector clocks show two writes are concurrent, not one-after-the-other) and hand both versions to the application or user to merge (Dynamo/Riak siblings) --- preserves both writes at the cost of merge logic. The staff decision hierarchy: **design for single-writer per datum whenever possible**; if you must multi-write, **use CRDTs where the data model allows**; fall back to **detect-and-merge (version vectors)** when semantics need it; and use **LWW only when silent loss of a concurrent write is acceptable**.

Follow: LWW seems fine --- newest wins. Show me exactly how it loses data and why clocks make it worse across regions.
Take two concurrent writes to the same key from two regions: region A sets `balance = 100` at its clock time T1, region B sets `balance = 120` at its clock time T2, and they replicate to each other. LWW keeps the one with the higher timestamp and **silently discards the other** --- so one of those two real, acknowledged updates just *disappears*, with no conflict surfaced and no record that it happened. That is already lossy for independent concurrent writes (both were legitimate; LWW pretends one did not occur). Across regions it gets worse because LWW depends on **comparing clocks from different machines**, and physical clocks skew --- region B's clock might be 200ms ahead, so B's write "wins" even though A's happened *later* in real time, and you discard the genuinely newer write. Worse, clock skew can make a *stale* write clobber a *fresh* one. Even with NTP, skew of tens of milliseconds is normal, which is the same order as replication lag, so the ordering LWW relies on is not trustworthy. The mitigations are to not use wall-clock LWW for anything you cannot afford to lose --- use **logical clocks / version vectors** to at least *detect* concurrency (so you know a conflict happened rather than silently dropping), or a system with **bounded clock uncertainty** (Spanner's TrueTime) if you truly need timestamp ordering. The Staff point is that LWW is not "newest wins," it is "**some** write wins and the other is lost, and across regions you cannot even trust which is newest."
Follow: CRDTs converge without loss --- so why not just use them for everything and stop worrying about conflicts?
Because CRDTs only exist for data whose operations **commute** or can be defined to merge losslessly, and most business data does not fit that mold without distorting its semantics. A counter (increments commute), a grow-only or add/remove set, a last-writer register, sequence CRDTs for text --- these have clean merge functions. But an operation like "**set balance to exactly 100**" or "book the *last* seat" or "enforce this unique constraint" has **no lossless merge** --- two regions both booking the last seat cannot both win, and there is no CRDT that makes an overbooking correct; the conflict is inherent in the *invariant*, not an artifact you can define away. CRDTs also carry costs: **metadata overhead** (tombstones for removed set elements, version data that grows), merge semantics that can be **surprising to users** (a delete losing to a concurrent update because the CRDT preserves both), and they push complexity into the data model. So CRDTs are the right tool for the slice of data that genuinely fits --- collaborative editing, counters, presence, carts --- and for **invariant-bearing data (balances, inventory, uniqueness) you fall back to single-writer ownership or a quorum/consensus write**, because those invariants require a single point of serialization that a merge function cannot provide. The judgment is matching the mechanism to whether the data's correctness tolerates a merge at all.
Senior: Being precise that **LWW is "some write wins, the other is silently lost, and cross-region clock skew makes 'newest' untrustworthy"**, and that **CRDTs only work for data whose operations merge losslessly --- so invariant-bearing data (balances, inventory, uniqueness) needs single-writer or consensus, not a CRDT** --- is the conflict-resolution depth that marks a Staff answer.
Speak: Give the hierarchy: **"first, avoid conflicts --- single writer per datum, and there's nothing to reconcile. If I must multi-write: CRDTs where the data merges losslessly (counters, sets, collaborative text), detect-and-merge with version vectors where semantics need both writes, and LWW only when silently losing one concurrent write is acceptable."** Then the sharp bit: LWW loses data and trusts skewed cross-region clocks, and no CRDT can make an overbooked seat correct --- invariants need a single writer.

### Staff | the failover decision: automatic vs manual

Should regional failover be automatic or manual, and what governs the choice?

It is a genuine trade between **RTO and the risk of a false failover**, and the answer depends on how confident your detection is and how costly a wrong failover is. **Automatic failover** hits a tight RTO (a machine reacts in seconds/minutes, no human in the loop) --- necessary when downtime must be minimal and a human cannot respond fast enough. But it is dangerous because **failover itself is disruptive and hard to reverse**: promoting a secondary, shifting DNS, and re-pointing writes are not instant to undo, so a **false positive** --- failing over on a transient network blip, a brief slowness, or a partial failure that would have self-healed --- causes an outage *you* triggered, and worse can cause **split-brain** if the "failed" primary is actually alive. It can also **flap** (failing back and forth) if the trigger is noisy. **Manual failover** (or "assisted": automation prepares, a human approves) avoids false positives and lets a human judge whether the region is *really* down and whether failing over is wise --- at the cost of a slower RTO (human response time, especially off-hours). What governs the choice: the **required RTO** (very tight -> lean automatic), the **reliability of detection** (can you confidently distinguish a real region failure from a blip? if not, automation is risky), the **blast radius and reversibility** of failover (cheap and safe -> automate; disruptive and hard to reverse -> gate it), and **fencing guarantees** (only automate if you can fence the old primary, or you risk split-brain). Mature practice: **automate detection and preparation but require confident, multi-signal triggers with conservative thresholds, always fence before promoting, and for the highest-stakes systems keep a human approval step**. The staff point: the cost of a *wrong* failover is often comparable to the outage you are protecting against, so you optimize not just for fast failover but for **not failing over when you shouldn't**.

Follow: Give me the decision rule --- when is automatic clearly right, and when is manual clearly right?
Automatic is clearly right when three things hold together: your **RTO is tighter than a human can meet** (seconds to low minutes, where paging someone is already too slow), your **detection is trustworthy** (you can distinguish a real region failure from a blip with high confidence --- multi-signal, multi-vantage, low false-positive rate), and **failover is safe and reversible** (you can fence the old primary, so a mistaken failover cannot cause split-brain, and failback is cheap). Stateless read-serving tiers behind a global load balancer are the easy yes --- health-check and shift, the "failover" is just routing. Manual (or assisted) is clearly right when the inverse holds: your **RTO tolerates a human** (tens of minutes is acceptable), your **detection is ambiguous** (partial failures, gray failures, "is it slow or down?"), or **failover is disruptive and hard to reverse** (promoting a stateful primary, re-homing writes, anything where a wrong call causes data divergence). The messy middle is the common case, and the mature pattern is **assisted failover**: automation continuously *detects and prepares* (pre-stages the promotion, keeps the standby hot) so the RTO is dominated by decision time, and a human provides the *judgment* to pull the trigger on a disruptive, hard-to-reverse action. You reserve fully-automatic for the safe, reversible, confidently-detected cases and human-gate the ones where a wrong failover is catastrophic.
Follow: You lean toward automatic for a tight RTO. What specific guardrails keep automatic failover from causing the disaster it is meant to prevent?
Five, and they compose. **Conservative, multi-signal detection**: multiple failed checks over a window, from multiple vantage points, on a real request path --- so you do not trigger on a single blip or a checker-to-region network glitch. **Mandatory fencing before promotion**: the new primary only comes up after the old one's writes are guaranteed rejected (stale token at the storage layer, or an expired lease), so an alive-but-partitioned primary cannot become a second writer --- this is the anti-split-brain guardrail and it is non-negotiable for automatic failover. **Anti-flapping / hysteresis**: once failed over, do not automatically fail *back* on a brief recovery signal --- require sustained health and ideally a human for failback --- so you do not oscillate. **A tripwire / circuit-breaker on the automation itself**: rate-limit failovers (if it is firing repeatedly, stop and page a human, because something is wrong with detection). And **observability + a fast manual override**: humans watching, with the ability to abort or force a failover, so the automation is supervised, not autonomous. The through-line is that automatic failover is only as safe as its ability to *not* fire wrongly and to *not* create two writers --- so the guardrails are all about confident triggering and guaranteed single-writer, not about failing over faster.
Senior: Framing the choice as a **decision rule over RTO-tightness, detection-confidence, and reversibility** (with assisted failover as the mature middle) and being able to name the **specific guardrails --- conservative multi-signal detection, mandatory fencing, anti-flap hysteresis, an automation circuit-breaker, and a manual override** --- rather than a flat "automate it," is the operational judgment a Staff round is checking.
Speak: Frame it as a trade, not a default: **"automatic hits a tight RTO but a false failover is an outage I caused --- and can cause split-brain if the primary was only partitioned. So I automate only when detection is confident and failover is reversible and fenced; otherwise I use assisted failover --- automation detects and prepares, a human approves the disruptive part."** Then name the guardrails: conservative multi-signal triggers, mandatory fencing before promote, and anti-flap hysteresis.

### Staff | cross-region consistency

What consistency can you realistically offer across regions, and how do you structure the system around it?

Realistically, **strong consistency is per-region (or per-owning-region), and cross-region is eventual** --- because guaranteeing global strong consistency requires cross-region coordination on the write path, which costs the ~100ms cross-region latency per write and reduces availability under partition (CAP/PACELC made physical by distance). So you structure the system to **contain strong-consistency needs within a region** and accept eventual consistency between regions. Concretely: **assign each datum a home region that is its source of truth and sole writer** (single-writer per datum), so within that region you can offer strong consistency (linearizable reads/writes locally), while other regions hold **asynchronously-replicated, eventually-consistent copies** for local reads. Operations that need strong consistency are routed to the home region (paying cross-region latency only when the caller is remote); operations that tolerate staleness read the local replica. For the rare truly-global strong-consistency requirement, you either use a system engineered for it (Spanner with TrueTime, at real latency/infra cost) or a quorum spanning regions (accepting the latency) --- reserved for data that genuinely cannot be eventually consistent. The design consequence is **you decide, per piece of data, its consistency and its home**: most data is single-writer-per-region with local eventually-consistent read replicas (strong where written, eventual elsewhere); a small set needing global agreement pays for cross-region consensus. You never claim "strong consistency everywhere," which would reintroduce the cross-region latency you built multi-region to avoid.

Follow: Explain PACELC here --- why is it a better lens than CAP for a multi-region system?
Because CAP only describes the **partition** case (during a partition, choose Consistency or Availability), but a multi-region system spends **almost all its time *not* partitioned**, and PACELC covers that steady state too: **if Partitioned, choose Availability or Consistency; Else (normal operation), choose Latency or Consistency**. The "else" half is the one that actually dominates your design, because even with no partition, offering strong consistency across regions *requires cross-region coordination on every such operation*, which **costs latency** (the ~100ms round trip) --- so you are trading Latency vs Consistency constantly, not just during the rare partition. A single-writer-per-region-with-async-replicas design is **PA/EL**: available under partition (the minority can still read its stale replica) and low-latency in normal operation (local reads, no cross-region wait), at the price of eventual consistency. A globally-strongly-consistent system like Spanner is **PC/EC**: consistent under partition and in normal operation, paying latency for it. PACELC is the better lens precisely because it forces you to price the **everyday latency-vs-consistency trade**, which is where multi-region actually lives, rather than only the once-a-year partition. Naming your system as PA/EL or PC/EC per datum is a more honest description than "CP or AP."
Follow: Spanner offers global strong consistency. Did it beat the CAP/PACELC trade, or just pay the price differently?
It **paid the price**, precisely and honestly --- it did not repeal physics. Spanner is a **CP / PC-EC** system: under a partition it chooses consistency and the minority side becomes unavailable for writes (Paxos groups need a quorum), and in normal operation it **pays latency for consistency** --- exactly the trades CAP and PACELC predict. What it engineered is not an escape but a way to make the consistency *affordable and correct*: **TrueTime**, an API that gives a *bounded* clock uncertainty (backed by GPS and atomic clocks in every datacenter) so it can assign globally meaningful commit timestamps, and it enforces ordering by **waiting out the uncertainty interval** (commit-wait) before making a write visible. That is where the latency shows up --- commit-wait plus cross-region Paxos --- so writes are slower and a partition still costs availability on the minority side. So Spanner's achievement is bounding clock uncertainty (which turns the impossible "agree on time globally" into a small, known wait) and building the infrastructure to keep that bound tight; it did not get global strong consistency for free, it **spent latency, availability-under-partition, and specialized hardware** to get it. The Staff framing is that "beat CAP" is always wrong --- Spanner chose CP and paid EC's latency, just with enough engineering that the price is tolerable for the workloads that need it.
Senior: Using **PACELC to price the everyday latency-vs-consistency trade** (not just CAP's partition case) and correctly stating that **Spanner did not beat the trade --- it is CP/EC and pays latency via TrueTime commit-wait plus specialized clocks** --- rather than mythologizing global strong consistency, is exactly the distributed-systems literacy a Staff round rewards.
Speak: Set the honest ceiling: **"strong consistency is per-region, cross-region is eventual --- because global strong consistency means cross-region coordination on the write path, which costs the ~100ms round trip. So I give each datum a home region that's its strong-consistent sole writer, and other regions hold async read replicas."** Then the lens: PACELC, because even without a partition I'm trading latency vs consistency on every cross-region operation --- and Spanner doesn't beat that, it pays it with TrueTime.

### Staff | traffic routing across regions

How do you route users to the right region, and steer traffic during a failover?

Through a **global traffic-routing layer** that decides, per request/client, which region to send it to --- and that same layer is what executes failover. The main mechanisms: **Geo-DNS / latency-based DNS** (a DNS service like Route 53 resolves your hostname to the nearest or lowest-latency healthy region based on the client's location/network) --- simple and widely used, but subject to **DNS caching/TTL** (clients cache the resolution, so changes propagate only as TTLs expire, which is why DR-critical records use low TTLs). **Anycast** (the same IP is announced from multiple regions, and BGP routes each client to the topologically-nearest one) --- used by global load balancers and CDNs; failover is fast because withdrawing a region's route reroutes traffic at the network layer without waiting for DNS TTLs. **Global load balancer** (a cloud global LB / edge network that terminates connections at the edge and routes to healthy regional backends, with built-in health checking) --- fast, health-aware routing and failover without client-side DNS caching issues. For **failover**, the routing layer health-checks regions and **stops sending traffic to an unhealthy one** (DNS failover updates the record; anycast/global-LB withdraws the failed region). Key considerations: **health checks** must be reliable and multi-vantage (to avoid false failover); **DNS TTL** must be low for anything you need to fail over quickly (high TTL = slow failover regardless of how fast you detect); **session/stickiness** must be handled (a user mid-session may need to move regions, and read-your-writes may require pinning them briefly after a write); and **anycast/global-LB generally beat raw geo-DNS for failover speed** because they do not depend on client DNS cache expiry. The staff framing: routing is both the **latency optimizer** (send each user to the nearest healthy region) and the **failover actuator** (withdraw an unhealthy region), and its failover speed is gated by the mechanism.

Follow: You set a low DNS TTL for fast failover. Why might traffic *still* not shift when you fail over, and what does that mean for your RTO?
Because **you do not control the resolvers and clients that cache your record**, and many of them ignore or extend your TTL. Some ISP and corporate resolvers **clamp TTLs to a minimum** (they will not honor a 30-second TTL and cache for minutes anyway), browsers and OSes do their own **DNS pinning/caching** beyond the TTL, and long-lived **connections** (an open HTTP/2 or database connection) do not re-resolve at all --- they keep talking to the dead region's IP until the connection breaks. So even with a 30-second TTL, a meaningful tail of clients keeps hitting the failed region for minutes, which means **DNS failover has a long, fat tail you cannot bound**, and your *effective* RTO for those clients is far worse than your TTL suggests. The implication: if you need a tight, *reliable* RTO, do not rely on DNS to move traffic --- use **anycast or a global load balancer**, where failover happens at the network/edge layer (withdraw a BGP announcement, or the LB stops routing to the region) and clients move in **seconds regardless of DNS caching**, because the same IP now lands them on a healthy region. DNS is fine for the coarse "which region" decision and for planned changes; it is the wrong tool for fast, guaranteed failover. The Staff point is that **your RTO is secretly capped by your slowest traffic-shift mechanism**, and DNS TTL is a floor you often cannot actually achieve.
Follow: A user is mid-session with an open connection and their region dies. Geo-DNS aside, what actually has to happen for them to recover?
Their **existing connection has to break and a new one has to be established to a healthy region** --- routing changes alone do not save an already-open connection pinned to a dead endpoint. Concretely: the client (or the edge/LB in front of it) must **detect the failure** (a timeout, a reset, a failed health check), **tear down** the dead connection, and **reconnect** --- at which point anycast/global-LB routes the new connection to a surviving region, or a fresh DNS resolution does (with the caching caveats). For this to be *fast and graceful*, the responsibility usually sits at a layer the user does not manage: a **global load balancer or edge proxy** terminates the client connection near the user and maintains its *own* connections to regional backends, so when a backend region dies the edge fails those over transparently and the client's connection to the edge survives. Absent that, you need **client-side resilience** --- sensible connection timeouts, retry-with-backoff on connection failure, and re-resolution on reconnect --- so the client itself moves. And any **session state** the user had in the dead region must be reconstructable from replicated/owned data in the surviving region (this is where the data model and read-your-writes come back in), or the user is bounced but lands somewhere that can serve them. So recovery for an in-flight user is connection teardown + reconnect + session continuity, which is why an edge/LB tier (that owns the client connection) plus replicated session data is what actually makes failover feel seamless, not DNS.
Senior: Knowing that **DNS TTL is a floor you often cannot achieve** (resolvers clamp it, clients pin, connections do not re-resolve) so a reliable tight RTO needs **anycast/global-LB**, and that an **in-flight user recovers only by connection teardown-and-reconnect** (best owned by an edge/LB tier plus replicated session state), is the routing-and-failover depth that separates a Staff answer from "we use Route 53."
Speak: Split routing's two jobs and gate them by mechanism: **"the routing layer both sends each user to the nearest healthy region and withdraws an unhealthy one on failover. Geo-DNS is simple but its failover speed is capped by DNS TTL --- which resolvers clamp and connections ignore --- so for a fast, reliable RTO I use anycast or a global LB that reroutes at the network layer in seconds."** Then note an in-flight user only recovers on connection teardown-and-reconnect, best owned by an edge tier.

### Staff | when NOT to go multi-region

When is multi-region the wrong choice, and what do you do instead?

Very often --- multi-region is a **large, permanent increase in cost and complexity**, and most systems do not need it, so the default should be **multi-AZ within a single region** unless a concrete requirement forces otherwise. Multi-region is the wrong choice when: your availability requirement is met by **multi-AZ** (which already survives a data-center failure --- the common failure mode --- cheaply, while a full region outage is rare); your users are **concentrated in one geography** (no latency case for distant regions); you have **no data-residency requirement**; or you have not yet exhausted **simpler resilience** (multi-AZ, good backups, automated in-region recovery). The costs you would take on unnecessarily: **doubled (or more) infrastructure spend**; **major complexity** (cross-region replication, conflict resolution or ownership, failover orchestration, split-brain prevention, DR testing) that adds risk and slows the team; **cross-region latency** intruding on the data model; and **operational burden** (game days, multi-region deploys, more failure modes). What to do instead: **multi-AZ** for data-center-failure resilience (the biggest resilience win per dollar), **robust backups + tested restore** and IaC for recovering from disasters (accepting a longer RTO than a hot standby, fine for many businesses), a **CDN/edge** for read latency to distant users without full multi-region backends, and only **escalate to multi-region when a real requirement appears** --- a strict RTO/RPO a single region cannot meet, genuinely global users needing low write latency, or data-residency law. The staff judgment: **name the specific requirement that justifies multi-region, and if you cannot, do not do it** --- because you would be paying double and taking on the hardest distributed-systems problems to solve something multi-AZ and backups already handle.

Follow: Your PM says "we should be multi-region for reliability." How do you push back without just saying no?
By **converting "reliability" into the specific numbers and failure mode it implies**, then showing which is the right-sized tool. I would ask: *what failure are we protecting against, and what RTO/RPO does the business actually require?* If the fear is "a data center fails," that is **multi-AZ**, which we can do far more cheaply and it handles the *common* case. If it is "a whole region fails," I would name that this is **rare**, and ask whether the business can tolerate, say, a few hours of recovery from backups --- if yes, **backups + IaC + tested restore** meets it at a fraction of multi-region's cost and complexity. Only if we need to survive a region loss with a **tight RTO/RPO a single region cannot meet** does multi-region become justified --- and then I would put the *cost* on the table: roughly double the infrastructure, plus the engineering time to build and operate cross-region replication, failover, and split-brain prevention, plus ongoing game days. Framed that way, the decision is the PM's to make with real trade-offs, and usually "reliability" turns out to mean "multi-AZ plus good backups," which we can deliver quickly. The move is not to refuse multi-region but to make the requirement and its price explicit, so we buy the resilience the business needs rather than the most impressive-sounding architecture.
Follow: Is there a cheaper way to survive a region failure than a full second live region --- something between multi-AZ and hot multi-region?
Yes --- **backup-and-restore or pilot-light DR into a second region**, which survives a region loss without running a full second live environment. The spectrum from cheap to expensive: **backups replicated cross-region** (your data and IaC exist in a second region; on a region loss you *provision and restore* there --- cheapest, RTO of hours, RPO as good as your replication/backup cadence); **pilot-light** (the data layer replicates live into the second region while compute sits near-zero, so RPO is good and RTO is the scale-up time --- moderate cost); and only then **warm/hot standby** and full active-active. So for "survive a region failure but we can tolerate a slower recovery," pilot-light or even cross-region backups give you the *survival* property for a small fraction of active-active's cost --- you are paying mainly for replicated storage, not a duplicated running fleet. The judgment is that "survive a region failure" does *not* automatically mean "run two live regions"; it means "have your data and a recovery path in a second region," and the RTO you require picks the tier. Most businesses that think they need multi-region actually need pilot-light DR --- current data in a second region and a tested, fast-enough recovery --- not a hot duplicate serving traffic.
Senior: Turning a vague "be multi-region for reliability" into the **specific failure mode and RTO/RPO**, pricing multi-region honestly (double infra + the hardest distributed problems + ongoing game days), and knowing the **cheaper middle (cross-region backups / pilot-light DR)** survives a region loss without a hot duplicate --- so you buy right-sized resilience --- is the restraint that reads as Staff.
Speak: Lead with restraint: **"most systems need multi-AZ, not multi-region --- multi-AZ handles the common data-center failure cheaply, and a region loss is rare. I'd only go multi-region for a strict RTO/RPO a single region can't meet, genuinely global latency, or residency law."** Then offer the middle: to survive a region loss without a hot duplicate, pilot-light or cross-region backups give you the survival property at a fraction of active-active's cost.

## Walk

### Why multi-region: DR, latency, residency

```flow
single[single region] -> risk[a region can fail, distant users lag, data must stay in-jurisdiction] -> multi[run across regions -- but pick which problem you are solving]
```

Start with the three distinct motivations, because they pull the design apart. **Disaster recovery**: a whole region can fail (a disaster, a large-scale outage, a region-wide control-plane failure), and a second region lets you survive it. **Latency**: distant users pay ~100ms+ each way to a single far region, so serving them locally is a big win. **Data residency**: law (GDPR, sovereignty) may force certain data to physically stay in a jurisdiction.

These do not want the same design --- DR wants a **standby you promote**, latency wants **all regions serving**, residency wants **data pinned per region** --- and multi-region is expensive and complex (running and coordinating copies of everything across high-latency links), so the first move is to name *which* problem you are solving. The motivation is not a formality: it decides whether you even have a distributed-data problem. Pure DR keeps one writer and replicates only for recovery; latency forces local reads, a write story, and always-on routing; residency turns the whole thing into a data-placement constraint. Get this wrong and you either over-build (active-active for a system that just needed DR) or under-build (a single-writer DR plan for a system that needed global latency).

### It is not multi-AZ --- the blast radius and the latency budget

```flow
az[multi-AZ: many data centers, one region] -> cheap[survives a DC failure -- sync replication, RPO ~0, nearly free] . region[multi-region: survives a whole-region failure -- forces async, RPO > 0]
```

Before going further, separate multi-AZ from multi-region, because they are different blast radii and confusing them is the fastest way to misstate what you are protected against. **Multi-AZ** runs across availability zones --- physically separate data centers within *one* region, each with independent power and networking but only single-digit-millisecond links apart. It survives a data-center failure (the common case), cheaply and as the standard. **Multi-region** survives a *whole-region* failure, which multi-AZ cannot, since all its AZs share the region.

The deeper point is that the AZ/region gap is a **latency budget**, not just a bigger circle. AZ-to-AZ round trips are ~1-2ms, so you can replicate **synchronously** across AZs and still return a fast write --- multi-AZ gives you an RPO of essentially *zero* nearly for free. Cross-region round trips are ~80-100ms, so synchronous replication would tax every write with that full latency; you are pushed to **async**, and immediately inherit a non-zero RPO and the possibility of conflicts. So multi-AZ is sync/RPO-zero/trivial, multi-region is async/RPO-positive, and *every* hard problem in this topic --- data loss on failover, split-brain, cross-region consistency --- follows from that one fact. Most systems only need multi-AZ.

### Topology: active-passive vs active-active

```flow
topo[topology?] -> ap[active-passive: one serves, a standby fails over -- simple, idle, failover delay] / aa[active-active: all serve -- low latency, no idle, but write conflicts]
```

**Active-passive**: one region serves, a warm/cold standby is kept replicated and promoted on failure. Simpler (one writer, no cross-region conflicts) but the standby is idle capacity, and failover has an RTO delay and an RPO data-loss window. The natural fit for **DR**. **Active-active**: all regions serve --- low latency everywhere, no idle capacity, graceful load redistribution on failure --- but multiple regions writing the same data creates **conflicts**, and there is no cheap strong global consistency. The fit for **global latency**, if you engineer the write story.

The topology is a *consequence* of the motivation, not a free choice, and there is a middle that most latency systems actually want. Active-passive's simplicity is real: a single writer means the consistency story stays trivial and there is nothing to reconcile, which is exactly why DR should not reach for active-active and its full multi-writer complexity. Active-active's "no idle capacity" is only true relative to a dark standby --- you still buy N/(N-1) capacity so the survivors can absorb a lost region --- and its low-latency promise comes bundled with the conflict problem. So the honest default for "multi-region for latency" is neither pure topology but **read-local, write-global**, which is the next step.

### Ownership: read-local, write-global

```flow
read[read] -> local[local replica -- fast] . write[write] -> owner[route to the datum's one owning region] -> repl[apply, then async-replicate out -- no conflict]
```

The middle ground most systems want: **replicate data to every region so reads are local**, but give each datum a single **owning region** that is its sole writer. Reads (the majority) are served from a nearby replica; a write is routed to the data's home region, which applies it and replicates it out asynchronously. Because only one region ever writes a given datum, there are **no cross-region write conflicts** to resolve --- you get local read latency everywhere while keeping writes conflict-free.

```python
def owner_region(entity):
    return entity.home_region              # each datum has ONE writer region

def route_write(entity, write):
    home = owner_region(entity)
    if current_region == home:
        apply(write)                       # local write, then replicate out async
    else:
        forward_to(home, write)            # cross-region hop to the owner -- no conflict
```

One writer per datum means nothing to reconcile; reads stay local and fast. You pay cross-region latency only on writes, and only when the writer is remote from the data's home --- so ownership should *track* the writer (a movable home, migrated on a sustained remote-write pattern), and a stale local read right after a remote write is repaired by pinning the writer to the home region for a short window, or by a version watermark the read waits on. Every hard question in this topic is a consequence of who is allowed to write.

### Cross-region data: async vs sync, and the RPO

```flow
data[cross-region write] -> sync[synchronous: wait for remote ack -- near-zero RPO, +100ms per write] / async[asynchronous: return now, ship in background -- low latency, lag = RPO]
```

The speed of light is the root problem: a cross-region round trip is ~80-100+ms (versus sub-ms in a data center). So **synchronous** replication --- not acking a write until another region confirms it --- gives a near-zero RPO but taxes *every* write with the full cross-region latency and blocks if the remote is unreachable. Almost always too expensive.

So you replicate **asynchronously**: commit and return in the origin region, ship the change in the background. That keeps writes fast but introduces a **replication lag**, and lag is what creates the two core problems --- **data loss on failover** (unreplicated writes vanish if the origin dies, so your RPO is roughly the lag) and **conflicts** (two regions can modify the same data in the lag window). You state an explicit **RTO** and **RPO** as targets and choose the standby tier and replication mode to hit them --- reserving synchronous for the narrow slice of data that can tolerate *no* loss and *can* pay the latency, or a semi-synchronous middle (sync to one nearby region, async to the rest) that guarantees two copies without waiting on the farthest region. And you *measure* lag continuously, because an RPO is a bound on the worst lag, not the average.

### Failover: detect, redirect, promote --- and fence

```flow
detect[health checks: region down?] -> redirect[global routing: withdraw the failed region] -> promote[drain backlog, flip to writable, FENCE the old primary -- your RTO]
```

Failover is detect -> redirect -> promote. **Detect** convincingly --- multiple failed checks over a short window, from multiple vantage points, because a false failover is an outage you caused. **Redirect** at a global routing layer --- anycast or a global LB for fast, TTL-independent failover; geo-DNS is bounded by TTL (which resolvers clamp and open connections ignore), so keep it low but do not rely on it for a tight RTO. **Promote** the standby: drain its replication backlog, flip its replica to a writable primary, and warm any cold capacity.

```python
def promote(standby, old_primary):
    standby.drain_replication_backlog()    # apply everything received but not yet replayed
    token = issue_fencing_token()          # monotonic: higher than the old primary's
    fence(old_primary, token)              # storage rejects any write with an older token
    standby.make_writable(token)           # only NOW does the new primary accept writes
    # data lost = writes in the replication lag window is your RPO
```

The order matters: you **fence before you promote**. Fencing does not require reaching the (possibly partitioned) old primary --- every write carries a monotonic token, and the storage layer rejects any write bearing a token older than the highest it has seen, so the old primary's writes are rejected the moment the new one takes a higher token. Skip the fence and an alive-but-unreachable primary keeps writing --- split-brain. The data you lose on promotion is exactly your RPO.

### Split-brain: the failure you design against

```flow
partition[partition: regions can't see each other] -> both[both keep writing the same datum?] / diverge[yes -> SPLIT-BRAIN: histories diverge irreconcilably] . safe[no: single-writer / quorum / fencing -> minority stops]
```

The failure to design against is **split-brain**: a partition cuts the regions off, but both keep writing, and their histories diverge irreconcilably --- and it is easy to cause by promoting a secondary while the primary is merely *unreachable, not dead*, giving you **two primaries**. It silently corrupts data because there is no single truth to reconcile to.

You prevent it structurally, three ways: **single-writer per datum** (the non-owner never writes it, even if it cannot see the owner --- read-local/write-global is inherently split-brain-proof); a **quorum** (writes need a majority, so a partitioned minority *cannot* form one and stops writing --- the Raft/Paxos model, which is deliberately choosing consistency over availability for the minority side); or **fencing** (revoke the old primary's write ability before promoting the new one). Which regime you pick is per-data: **CP** (quorum/single-writer, minority unavailable) where divergence is catastrophic, versus a planned **AP** design (both write, reconcile on heal with CRDTs or version vectors) where availability matters more and the data can converge. Split-brain is only a *bug* in a system that wanted consistency but failed to enforce a single writer.

### Prove it: RTO/RPO and game days

```flow
plan[a DR plan on paper] -> assume[is an assumption -- replication rots, TTLs too high, scripts untested] -> gameday[fail over for real, measure RTO/RPO -> only NOW is it DR]
```

The last step is the one people skip: **an untested DR plan is not a plan, it is an assumption**. Multi-region failover is a complex, rarely-exercised procedure that quietly rots --- replication silently breaks, DNS TTLs are too high to shift traffic in your RTO, the standby is under-provisioned, a secret exists only in the primary, the promotion script has a bug --- and none of it surfaces until you need it.

So you run **game days**: on a schedule, deliberately fail over to the secondary, execute the *actual* procedure, and **measure the real RTO and RPO** against your targets, verifying the secondary serves correct traffic under representative load. You test the *full* path (detect, shift, promote, fail back), you run it as a controlled failover with rehearsed rollback so it is safe, and you re-run it regularly because DR rots as the system changes around it. The stance: you do not *have* DR until you have failed over under test and hit your numbers --- and the first real failover should never be during an actual disaster. Motivation, topology, async data + RTO/RPO, split-brain/fencing, proof --- and the restraint to prefer multi-AZ unless a real requirement forces multi-region.

### Ownership --- who is allowed to write each datum?

**Exactly one region per datum** (its home). Everyone else holds an async read replica. Route a write to the owning region; it applies and replicates out. One writer means **zero conflicts to reconcile**, and reads stay local. Ownership must *track* the writer (a movable home), and a stale local read right after a remote write is fixed by pinning the writer to the home region briefly, or a version watermark the read waits on.

### Replication --- how does data cross regions?

**Asynchronously**, because the ~100ms cross-region round trip makes synchronous replication too expensive on every write. Async keeps writes fast but introduces a **replication lag**, and that lag is your **RPO** (data lost if the origin dies) and the window in which conflicts can form. Reserve **synchronous** for the narrow no-loss slice, or use semi-sync (sync to one nearby region, async to the rest).

### The numbers --- RTO and RPO

**RTO** = how fast you recover (bought with standby warmth and automation, paid in idle capacity and failover risk). **RPO** = how much data you can lose (bought with replication, paid in write latency; roughly your replication lag). An RPO is a bound on the **worst** lag, not the average --- so you alarm on lag, because a lag above your RPO means your DR promise is already broken.

### Failover --- what happens when the primary dies?

The data lost on promotion is exactly your RPO.

### Split-brain --- what happens when the regions can't see each other?

A partition risks **split-brain**: both regions keep writing and their histories **diverge irreconcilably** on heal. Prevent it structurally --- **single-writer ownership** (the non-owner never writes it), a **quorum** (a minority can't reach a majority, so it stops writing), or **fencing** (revoke the old primary's write ability before promoting). It is a CP-vs-AP choice per datum: quorum/single-writer where divergence is catastrophic, planned AP-with-merge where availability wins.

### Proof --- how do you know your DR actually works?

You do not, until you have **failed over under a game day and hit your RTO/RPO**. An untested plan is an assumption: replication silently breaks, TTLs are too high, scripts have bugs, secrets live only in the primary. So you run scheduled, controlled failovers, measure the real numbers, test the full path, and re-run regularly because DR rots as the system changes.

Foot: **The one people forget:** the fence. A shakier candidate says "we just promote the standby," and the interviewer hears "would cause split-brain and corrupt the data" --- because promoting a secondary while the old primary is only *unreachable, not dead* leaves two writers. Fence the old primary (a monotonic token the storage layer enforces) *before* you promote, and an alive-but-partitioned region cannot come back as a second writer.

Verdict: name the motivation (DR / latency / residency) -> pick topology (active-passive for DR, read-local/write-global for latency) -> one owner per datum, replicate async with explicit RTO/RPO -> prevent split-brain with single-writer, quorum, or fencing-before-promote -> and prefer multi-AZ unless a real requirement forces multi-region. **All eight cold, and the multi-region round is yours to lose, not to pass.**

## System

Multi-region sits **underneath everything** --- it is not a component you add but a property of the whole deployment. The motivation picks the topology, the topology picks the data model, and the data model decides what a failover and a partition do to you. The whole design falls out of one physical fact: the speed of light forces async replication, async forces an RPO and the possibility of conflicts, and a partition plus a promotion forces split-brain unless you fence. Lead with that causal chain, not the box diagram.

### Where it sits in the design

Motivation: DR (survive a region) / latency (serve locally) / residency (pin data) -- decides everything downstream
Topology: active-passive (DR) vs active-active vs read-local/write-global -- single owner per datum [*]
Cross-region data: async replication (lag = RPO) is the default; sync only for no-loss data (~100ms/write)
Traffic routing: geo-DNS / anycast / global LB -- health-checked; DNS TTL caps how fast failover shifts traffic
Failover: detect -> redirect -> promote (fence the old primary first); RTO and RPO stated explicitly
Split-brain prevention: single-writer ownership, quorum (minority can't write), or fencing before promotion

### Pivots an interviewer rides

From "make it multi-region" they push on the data model and the failure modes --- each pivot leads into a deeper answer. Tap to see the connecting reasoning.

#### Active-passive or active-active --- how do you choose?

-> active-passive for DR; active-active for latency
DR does not need the standby to serve, so **active-passive** is far simpler --- one writer, no cross-region conflicts, a clean consistency story. Low global latency needs local serving, so **active-active** --- but you almost always route each datum's writes to a single owning region (**read-local/write-global**) to eliminate the multi-writer conflict problem entirely, and you provision N-1 capacity so a lost region does not overload the survivors. The topology is a consequence of the motivation, not a free choice.

#### How do you prevent split-brain on failover?

-> fence the old primary before promoting, or quorum
Never promote a secondary while the primary might be **alive-but-unreachable** without fencing it --- revoke its write ability via a monotonic token the storage layer enforces (it rejects any write with an older token), so you do not have to reach the partitioned node. Or require writes to hold a **majority quorum**, so the minority side of a partition structurally cannot accept writes and stops on its own. And detect conservatively (multiple signals, multiple vantage points) so you do not fail over on a blip and cause the split-brain you are trying to avoid.

#### Async or sync replication --- how much data can you lose?

-> async (lag = RPO) default; sync for no-loss
You replicate **asynchronously** because the ~100ms cross-region round trip makes synchronous replication too expensive on every write --- and you accept that the replication lag *is* your **RPO** (the writes you lose if the origin region dies mid-lag: lag times write-rate is the concrete count). You reserve **synchronous** (or semi-sync to one nearby region) for the narrow slice of data that can tolerate no loss and can pay the per-write latency. And you alarm on lag, because an RPO is a bound on the worst lag, not the average.

#### How do you route users and steer a failover?

-> anycast / global LB; DNS TTL caps failover speed
A **global routing layer** sends each user to the nearest healthy region and withdraws an unhealthy one on failover. **Geo-DNS** is simple but its failover speed is capped by **DNS TTL** --- which resolvers clamp, clients pin, and open connections ignore --- so for a fast, reliable RTO you use **anycast or a global load balancer** that reroutes at the network layer in seconds. An in-flight user with an open connection only recovers on connection teardown-and-reconnect, best owned by an edge tier plus replicated session data.

#### What does data residency change?

-> pin regulated data per jurisdiction; replicate within-region
Residency turns multi-region from an availability/latency choice into a **data-placement constraint**: EU users' regulated data must live in and stay in the EU, so you **partition users by home region**, replicate only within-jurisdiction (including for DR --- the standby for EU data is another EU region), and route a roaming user's writes back to their home region rather than persisting them abroad. Global views are built by **querying per-region and aggregating**, or replicating only a non-regulated projection --- never by copying regulated data out.

#### Do you even need multi-region?

-> usually multi-AZ + backups is the right answer
Often not. **Multi-AZ** already survives a data-center failure (the common case) cheaply, and a whole-region outage is rare. So the default is multi-AZ plus **tested backups** and a **CDN** for read latency, and you only escalate to multi-region for a strict RTO/RPO a single region cannot meet, genuinely global users needing low write latency, or residency law. To survive a region loss without a hot duplicate, **pilot-light DR** (data replicating live, compute near-zero) gives the survival property for a fraction of active-active's cost. Name the specific requirement, or do not do it.

## Trade-offs

The calls that separate "we have a second region" from a DR design that actually fails over and does not diverge --- each with the **axis** that picks a side. The tell that sinks you is defending one topology or one replication mode as universally right; the honest answer is always about the RTO, RPO, and latency the business actually committed to, and the cheapest design that hits them.

### Active-passive vs active-active

- Active-passive: **disaster recovery** is the goal --- one region serves, a replicated standby fails over. Simple (one writer, no cross-region conflicts, clean consistency), a clean standby to promote --- but idle standby capacity, a failover delay (RTO), a data-loss window (RPO), and no latency benefit.
- Active-active: **global low latency** is the goal --- all regions serve, no idle capacity, graceful failure redistribution --- but concurrent cross-region writes create conflicts, there is no cheap strong global consistency, and the ownership/ops are far more complex.

Active-passive for DR (much simpler, and DR does not need the standby to serve); active-active for genuinely global low-latency needs --- but reach first for **read-local/write-global** (single owner per datum), which gives local reads without the multi-writer conflict problem, and remember active-active only fails over fast if the survivors carry N-1 capacity.

### Async vs sync cross-region replication

- Async: the **practical default** given ~100ms cross-region RTT --- commit and return, replicate in the background. Low write latency --- but a replication lag that is your RPO (data loss on failover) and allows stale reads and conflicts.
- Sync: **no-loss-critical data** --- a write is not acked until another region confirms it. Near-zero RPO and stronger consistency --- but every write pays the full cross-region round trip (~100ms+) and writes block if the remote region is unreachable.

Async for essentially everything, with a well-understood, alarmed RPO; sync only for the narrow slice that can tolerate no loss and can pay the per-write latency --- or **semi-sync** (sync to one nearby region, async to the rest) for two-copy durability without waiting on the farthest region. Most multi-region systems are async because 100ms per write globally is rarely acceptable.

### Single-writer ownership vs multi-master writes

- Single-writer per datum: the datum has **one natural home** --- route its writes there, and concurrent conflicting writes are structurally impossible. No conflict resolution, no reconciliation --- but a write from elsewhere pays a cross-region hop to the owner.
- Multi-master (write anywhere): the datum has **no single owner** and must be written from multiple regions concurrently (a global counter, collaborative text, inventory) --- lower write latency everywhere, but you now owe a conflict strategy and accept eventual consistency.

Default to single-writer ownership --- it is the cleanest answer because there is nothing to reconcile. Go multi-master only where the data genuinely cannot be single-owned, and then use **CRDTs** where the data merges losslessly (counters, sets, sequences), **detect-and-merge** (version vectors) where semantics need both writes, and **LWW only when silently losing one concurrent write is acceptable** --- never for invariant-bearing data like balances or inventory.

### Automatic vs manual failover

- Automatic: a **tight RTO** where a human cannot respond fast enough --- a machine reacts in seconds. But a false positive is an outage you triggered, and can cause split-brain if the "failed" primary is alive; it can also flap on a noisy trigger.
- Manual / assisted: **ambiguous detection or a disruptive, hard-to-reverse failover** --- a human applies judgment about whether the region is really down. Avoids false positives, at the cost of slower recovery (human response time).

Automate only when detection is confident (multi-signal, multi-vantage), failover is reversible, and you can **fence** the old primary; otherwise use **assisted failover** --- automation detects and prepares, a human approves the disruptive part. The cost of a *wrong* failover is often comparable to the outage you are protecting against, so optimize for not failing over when you should not, not just for failing over fast.

### Geo-DNS vs anycast / global load balancer

- Geo-DNS: **simple, planned routing** --- resolve the hostname to the nearest healthy region. Widely used and easy --- but failover speed is capped by DNS TTL, which resolvers clamp, clients pin, and open connections ignore, so failover has a long, un-boundable tail.
- Anycast / global LB: **fast, reliable failover** --- the same IP announced from multiple regions (BGP routes to the nearest), or an edge LB with health-checked backends. Reroutes at the network layer in seconds, independent of DNS caching --- but more infrastructure.

Geo-DNS is fine for the coarse "which region" decision and planned changes; for a tight, *reliable* RTO use anycast or a global LB, because your RTO is secretly capped by your slowest traffic-shift mechanism, and DNS TTL is a floor you often cannot actually achieve. An in-flight user still only recovers on reconnect, so an edge tier that owns the client connection is what makes failover feel seamless.

### Multi-region vs multi-AZ

- Multi-AZ (one region, many AZs): survives a **data-center failure** (the common case) cheaply and simply, negligible added latency, synchronous replication so RPO ~0, no conflict or split-brain problems --- but does not survive a whole-region outage.
- Multi-region: survives a **full region loss**, enables global low latency, meets residency --- but doubles infra cost and adds the hardest distributed problems (conflicts, failover, split-brain, DR testing).

Multi-AZ is the right answer for most systems --- biggest resilience per dollar, handles the common failure. Escalate to multi-region only for a strict RTO/RPO a single region cannot meet, genuinely global users, or data-residency law --- and pair multi-AZ with tested backups and a CDN, or a pilot-light second region, before reaching for a hot multi-region duplicate.

### The split-brain trap | "You fail over automatically, but the primary was only partitioned and is still serving. Now what?"

The classic trap: two primaries diverge. Fence before promote, or use quorum so the minority self-stops.

- FRAME | frame | That's the classic **split-brain trap**, and it's exactly why fencing is non-negotiable. If I promoted the secondary without fencing the primary, I now have **two primaries taking writes**, and when the partition heals their histories have diverged with no single truth to reconcile to --- silent data corruption.
- THE ROOT CAUSE | head | The root cause is that my detection couldn't distinguish **"the region is dead" from "the region is unreachable from me."** A partition between my monitoring and the primary looks identical to a primary failure, so I failed over on ambiguous evidence, and the still-alive primary kept accepting writes.
- FENCE | sub | The structural fix is **fencing**: I only ever promote after revoking the old primary's write ability. Every write carries a **monotonic token**, and the storage layer rejects any write with a token older than the highest it's seen. When the new primary takes a higher token, the old primary's writes are rejected --- even though it's alive and thinks it's in charge, and even though I can't reach it to tell it to stop.
- OR QUORUM | sub | The alternative is a **quorum** model: writes require a majority, so the partitioned-off minority structurally can't form one and stops accepting writes on its own. That's the Raft/Paxos approach --- it deliberately chooses consistency over availability for the minority side, which is right when divergence is catastrophic.
- DETECT BETTER | sub | And I'd make detection **conservative and multi-signal** --- multiple failed checks from multiple vantage points --- and for a stateful primary, often a **human approval gate**, because the cost of a wrong failover is as bad as the outage I'm protecting against.
- NAME THE RISK | risk | The thing I'd resist is "just add a health check and promote faster" --- that makes the trap *more* likely, not less. Speed without fencing is how a five-minute blip becomes permanent data divergence. The guarantee has to be single-writer, enforced, not "we're pretty sure the old one is dead."
- CLOSE | close | So concretely, right now: I stop, I confirm whether the primary is actually dead or just partitioned, and I **fence before I promote** --- or I rely on a quorum where the minority already stopped itself. Fence-before-promote is the rule; failing over fast without it is how you cause the disaster.

### Async and the RPO | "How does data cross regions, and what exactly do you lose?"

Async because sync pays 100ms per write; the lag is your RPO, which is a count of writes you can lose.

- FRAME | frame | Cross-region data is the hard part, and it's the part people hand-wave by saying it "just syncs." The honest answer starts with physics: a cross-region round trip is **~80-100ms**, versus sub-millisecond in a data center, and that one fact drives everything.
- WHY ASYNC | head | So **synchronous** replication --- not acking a write until another region confirms it --- gives a near-zero RPO but taxes *every* write with that full ~100ms round trip, and blocks writes entirely if the remote region is unreachable. That's almost always too expensive, so I replicate **asynchronously**: commit and return locally, ship the change in the background.
- THE RPO | sub | The cost of async is a **replication lag**, and the lag **is my RPO** --- if the origin region dies before replicating, everything in the lag window is gone. And I'd make that concrete: lag times write-rate is a *count*. A "3-second RPO" at 5,000 writes a second is **fifteen thousand acknowledged writes** I could lose, which is a very different conversation than "a few seconds."
- ALARM ON LAG | sub | Because an RPO is a bound on the lag **at the moment of failure**, not the average, I measure lag continuously and **alarm when it exceeds the RPO** --- lag spikes on exactly the events that precede an incident, a load surge or a bulk backfill, so a lag graph nobody watches means my RPO is a hope, not a guarantee.
- THE MIDDLE | sub | If some data genuinely can't lose anything, I don't go full-sync everywhere --- I use **semi-sync** (synchronous to one nearby region, async to the rest) so an ack means two copies without waiting on the farthest region, or I make just that **critical subset** synchronous while everything else stays async.
- NAME THE RISK | risk | The trap in semi-sync is the **silent downgrade** --- some systems fall back to async when the sync follower is slow, so under the exact load spike where I need the guarantee, I quietly lose it. So I alarm on the *downgrade event itself*, not just configure the timeout.
- CLOSE | close | So: async by default because sync's per-write latency is unaffordable, the lag is my RPO expressed as a count of writes at risk, I alarm on lag continuously, and I reserve sync or semi-sync for the narrow no-loss slice --- watching for the silent downgrade. Never "it just syncs."

- HIT THE RPO | head | To hit **RPO 1 minute**, I replicate the data layer **asynchronously with sub-minute lag**, and I monitor that lag with an alarm if it exceeds a minute --- because the RPO is only real if I know the lag is under it at the moment of failure, not on average.
- HIT THE RTO | sub | To hit **RTO 15 minutes**, the standby must be at least **warm**, or pilot-light with fast scale-up --- data always replicating and compute pre-provisioned or quickly scalable. A cold backup-restore can't recover in 15 minutes, so the tier is chosen by the RTO.
- FAILOVER | sub | Failover: **multi-vantage health checks** detect a real region outage conservatively, a global routing layer or low-TTL DNS shifts traffic, and the standby's replica is promoted --- with the **old primary fenced before promotion** to prevent split-brain. - PROVE IT | sub | Critically, I'd run scheduled **game days** that actually execute the failover and **measure real RTO and RPO** against the 15-minute and 1-minute targets --- adjusting standby warmth and replication lag until they're met --- as controlled failovers with rehearsed rollback, because the plan isn't real until tested.

So the game days have to be **regular**, not a one-time certification; a six-month-old pass certifies a system that no longer exists.
- CLOSE | close | So: active-passive because it's DR, async replication with sub-minute monitored lag for the RPO, a warm standby with automated fenced failover for the RTO, and regular game days that measure the real numbers. Right-sized for 15-and-1, and proven, not assumed.

### Name the limits | "Where does this design fall short?"

The physics you can't cheat: an RPO you can't drive to zero cheaply, eventual cross-region consistency, split-brain risk, and DR that rots.

- FRAME | frame | Four limits I'd name up front, each with why it's a limit and when it bites --- because naming where the design bends is how I show I understand it isn't magic.
- THE RPO FLOOR | head | **I can't drive the RPO to zero cheaply.** Async replication always leaves a lag window, and closing it means synchronous replication that taxes every write with the full cross-region latency. So there's a genuine floor: a small, non-zero RPO is the price of affordable writes, and only a narrow critical slice justifies paying sync's latency to erase it.
- EVENTUAL CONSISTENCY | sub | **Cross-region consistency is eventual, not strong.** Strong consistency is per-owning-region; globally it would require cross-region coordination on every write, which costs the ~100ms round trip --- PACELC's latency-vs-consistency trade, which I'm paying even when there's no partition. Users can see stale reads across regions, and I mitigate read-your-writes with pinning or version watermarks, but I can't offer cheap global strong consistency.
- SPLIT-BRAIN | sub | **Split-brain is a permanent risk, not a solved problem.** Fencing and quorum prevent it, but they depend on correct implementation --- a promotion path that skips the fence, or a detection bug, reintroduces it. The guarantee is only as good as its enforcement, which is why I make single-writer structural and test failover.
- DR ROT | sub | **DR rots as the system changes.** A plan that passed six months ago certifies a system that no longer exists --- new services, unreplicated secrets, lag creep. So the guarantee is freshness-sensitive: it's only as good as my last successful, representative game day.
- HONEST CLOSE | trade | None of these is a reason not to build it --- they're the things I'd monitor and sequence: alarm on lag for the RPO, watermarks for read-your-writes, structural fencing for split-brain, and regular game days for rot. And the biggest limit is upstream: for most systems, the right answer is multi-AZ plus backups, and multi-region is the wrong tool.
- CLOSE | close | So the limits are a non-zero RPO floor, eventual cross-region consistency, split-brain that's prevented-not-eliminated, and DR that rots --- each bounded, each watched, none a surprise. And the honest meta-limit is that multi-region is over-built for most systems, so the first question is always whether we need it at all.

## Numbers

Back-of-envelope the two numbers you actually commit to: the **RPO** that asynchronous replication hands you, and the **RTO floor** your DNS TTL secretly imposes. Both come straight from physics --- the cross-region round trip and the replication lag --- so you can compute them instead of hand-waving.

Lead with the **data-loss window**: RPO is roughly replication lag times the write rate, which turns "our RPO is a few seconds" into a concrete count of lost writes. Then price the alternative: synchronous replication instead adds the full cross-region round trip to *every* write, and DNS-based failover can shift traffic no faster than your TTL --- and usually slower, because resolvers clamp it and open connections ignore it.

- rtt | Cross-region round-trip (ms) | 90 | 1 | 5
- lag | Async replication lag (seconds) | 3 | 0 | 1
- wps | Writes per second | 5000 | 0 | 500
- ttl | Failover DNS TTL (seconds) | 60 | 0 | 10

## Red Flags

The moves that quietly tank a multi-region round. Each is something a shakier candidate actually says, what the interviewer hears, and the line that flips it --- and most of them trace back to treating multi-region as "run the same system in two places" rather than as a distributed-data problem.

### "We're multi-region --- we run in three availability zones"

Multi-AZ and multi-region are **different blast radii**: three AZs are all in one region and survive a data-center failure, but not a whole-region outage --- so calling multi-AZ "multi-region" misstates what you are protected against. The interviewer hears *"doesn't know what failure they're actually surviving,"* which undercuts everything that follows.

Be precise: **multi-AZ** (cheap, standard, single-digit-ms links so you can replicate synchronously with RPO ~0) survives a data-center failure; **multi-region** (expensive, complex, ~100ms links so you are forced to async with RPO > 0) survives a whole-region failure and enables global latency or residency --- and name which one the requirement actually needs. Most systems only need multi-AZ.

### "On failure we just promote the standby to primary"

Promoting a secondary while the old primary might be **alive-but-unreachable** creates split-brain --- two primaries take writes and diverge irreconcilably when the partition heals, silently corrupting data with no single truth to reconcile to. The interviewer hears *"would cause split-brain and corrupt the data,"* which is the fastest no-hire in this topic.

**Fence the old primary before promoting** --- revoke its write ability via a monotonic fencing token the storage layer enforces (it rejects any write with an older token), so even an alive-but-partitioned primary cannot resume writing --- or use a **quorum** so a partitioned minority structurally cannot write. And detect the failure conservatively (multiple signals, multiple vantage points) so you do not fail over on a transient blip in the first place.

### "We'll replicate synchronously across regions so we never lose data"

Synchronous cross-region replication adds the full **~100ms round trip to every write** and **blocks writes when the remote region is unreachable** --- usually unacceptable, and it reintroduces the very latency you may have gone multi-region to avoid. The interviewer hears *"would tax every user forever to solve a rare-event problem."*

Replicate **asynchronously** with an explicit, alarmed RPO for essentially everything, and reserve synchronous replication for the narrow slice of data that can tolerate no loss and can pay the per-write latency --- or use **semi-sync** (sync to one nearby region, async to the rest) for two-copy durability without waiting on the farthest region. Accept a small, measured data-loss window rather than taxing every write.

### "Concurrent writes conflict, so we use last-write-wins"

Last-write-wins does not resolve conflicts, it **silently discards one of two real writes** --- and across regions it decides the "winner" by comparing **clocks from different machines**, which skew by tens of milliseconds, so a *stale* write can clobber a *fresh* one. The interviewer hears *"loses acknowledged data and trusts unsynchronized clocks."*

First, **avoid** conflicts: give each datum a single owning region so concurrent writes to it are structurally impossible. Where you genuinely must multi-write, use **CRDTs** where the data merges losslessly (counters, sets, sequences), or **version vectors** to detect concurrency and merge both writes --- and reserve LWW for data where silently losing a concurrent write is truly acceptable. No merge function can make an overbooked seat correct, so invariant-bearing data needs a single writer.

### "We have a disaster-recovery runbook"

A DR plan that has never been **exercised** is an assumption, not a capability --- replication silently breaks, DNS TTLs are too high to shift traffic in your RTO, the standby is under-provisioned, a secret exists only in the primary, the promotion script has a bug --- and none of it surfaces until the disaster. The interviewer hears *"has a plan on paper and no idea if it works."*

**Run game days**: on a schedule, deliberately fail over, execute the *actual* procedure, and **measure the real RTO and RPO** against your targets, as a controlled failover with rehearsed rollback. Re-run them regularly, because DR rots as the system changes around it. You do not *have* DR until you have failed over under test and hit your numbers --- and the first real failover should never be during an actual disaster.

### "Failover is fully automatic --- it reacts in seconds"

Automatic failover on a **twitchy trigger** is dangerous: a transient blip, or a partition between your monitor and the region, looks identical to a region failure, so you fail over on a false positive --- an outage *you* caused --- and if the "failed" primary is alive, you get split-brain. The interviewer hears *"would flap and self-inflict outages, and hasn't weighed the cost of a wrong failover."*

Automate only when detection is **confident** (multiple failed checks, multiple vantage points, a real request path), failover is **reversible**, and you can **fence** the old primary; otherwise use **assisted failover** --- automation detects and prepares, a human approves the disruptive part. The cost of a wrong failover is comparable to the outage you are protecting against, so optimize for *not* failing over when you should not, with conservative thresholds and anti-flap hysteresis.

### "We use geo-DNS, so failover is basically instant"

DNS failover is capped by **TTL**, and worse, you do not control the resolvers and clients that cache your record --- ISPs **clamp low TTLs**, clients **pin** DNS, and open connections **do not re-resolve at all** --- so a meaningful tail keeps hitting the dead region for minutes. The interviewer hears *"thinks a low TTL guarantees a fast failover, and hasn't hit this in production."*

For a tight, *reliable* RTO, do not rely on DNS to move traffic: use **anycast or a global load balancer**, where failover happens at the network/edge layer (withdraw a route, or the LB stops routing to the region) and clients move in seconds regardless of DNS caching. DNS is fine for the coarse "which region" decision; it is the wrong tool for fast failover, because your RTO is secretly capped by your slowest traffic-shift mechanism.

### "We went multi-region because it's more reliable"

Multi-region is a **large, permanent tax** --- roughly double the infrastructure, plus cross-region replication, conflict handling, failover orchestration, split-brain prevention, and ongoing game days --- and for most systems it solves a problem **multi-AZ and backups already handle** more cheaply. The interviewer hears *"reached for the most impressive architecture instead of right-sizing the resilience."*

Name the **specific requirement** that justifies it --- a strict RTO/RPO a single region cannot meet, genuinely global users needing low write latency, or residency law --- and if you cannot, do **multi-AZ plus tested backups plus a CDN** instead, or a **pilot-light** second region to survive a region loss without a hot duplicate. Match the resilience investment to the actual RTO/RPO and latency the business committed to, and reach for multi-region last.

Note: This one reads as diligent --- more regions *sounds* like more reliability --- but it is the "expensive by default" trap: you take on the hardest distributed-systems problems (conflicts, split-brain, failover) and double the bill to protect against a *rare* whole-region outage, while the *common* failure (a data center) was already covered far more cheaply by multi-AZ. Reliability is a set of specific RTO/RPO numbers, not a topology you adopt to look robust.

## Opener

### Match the altitude | The same multi-region design, said three ways

Interviewers open with *"quickly, how would you make this multi-region?"* as often as *"design a globally-distributed system."* Give the altitude they asked for --- the motivation when they want the motivation, the mechanism when they want mechanism --- then expand only when they pull. Say each out loud before you reveal mine.

#### **One breath.** The whole design in a single sentence --- for *"high level"* or *"quickly."*

Running across regions for disaster recovery, latency, or residency, dominated by two coupled decisions: the **topology** (active-passive for DR, active-active or read-local/write-global for latency) and **how data crosses regions** (async with a lag that is your RPO, or sync with a latency tax) --- with split-brain and RTO/RPO as the outcomes you commit to.

#### **Thirty seconds.** What you lead with, unprompted --- the load-bearing ideas, no diagram.

First I'd pin the **motivation** --- DR, latency, or residency --- because they pull the design apart, and I'd note this is not multi-AZ (a whole-region blast radius versus a data-center one). Then the **topology** follows: active-passive for DR, but for latency I'd reach for **read-local, write-global** --- replicate everywhere for local reads, but route each datum's writes to one owning region, so there are no conflicts to reconcile. Cross-region I replicate **asynchronously**, because ~100ms per write synchronously isn't viable, so the replication lag *is* my **RPO**, and I'd commit to explicit RTO and RPO. The failure I design against is **split-brain** --- I prevent it by fencing the old primary before promoting, or a quorum --- and I'd prove the whole thing with **game days**. And I'd challenge whether multi-region is even needed: multi-AZ plus backups handles most resilience.

- "split-brain" | the partition-plus-promotion failure, and fencing vs quorum | Whiteboard &middot; Probe Drill

## Bank

### FRAME | "Take this single-region service global --- low latency for users worldwide, and survival of a whole-region outage. Start wherever you like."

Task: Frame the scope in one line, then give your one-sentence design.
Model: **Frame:** we're solving two motivations at once --- latency for a global user base and DR for a region loss --- and I'll name that, because it pushes me toward all-regions-serving rather than a dark standby. **One-liner:** replicate data to every region for local reads, give each user's data a single owning region as its sole writer so there are no write conflicts (read-local/write-global), replicate asynchronously so the lag is my RPO, route users via anycast or a global LB, and on a region loss the survivors absorb the load while I fence-and-promote any single-owner data whose home died --- preventing split-brain with single-writer ownership plus fencing.
Int: How do you keep a user's writes conflict-free while their reads are local everywhere?
Single-writer per datum: I replicate for local reads everywhere, but designate one owning region as the only writer for each user's data and route their writes there. Because only one region ever writes a given datum, concurrent conflicting writes are structurally impossible --- so I never reconcile anything. I pay cross-region latency only on writes, and only when the user is away from their home region, and I use read-your-writes pinning (or a version watermark) so they don't read a stale local replica right after a remote write.
Int2: Two regions each run at 60% CPU. One region fails. What happens?
The survivor has to absorb the dead region's traffic on top of its own --- 60% plus 60% is 120%, so it falls over, and my "graceful" failover becomes a cascading total outage. That's the N-1 capacity trap: active-active only fails over gracefully if the survivors can carry 100% of traffic, so at two regions each must run at 50% or less --- which means I'm buying 2x capacity, the same bill as an idle standby, just spread out and doing useful work. At three regions each can run at ~66%, so the marginal cost of resilience falls as I add regions. Autoscaling doesn't save me here: it takes minutes exactly when I'm already degraded, the capacity may not be available, and the new fleet has cold caches that hammer the database.

### STRUCTURE | "Walk me through what happens to a single write, end to end, in your design."

Task: Trace one write from the client to durable, replicated state --- no hand-waving "it syncs."
Model: The client's write lands on the **nearest region**, but that region checks **who owns this datum**. If it's the owning region, it **applies the write locally** (a fast, local commit) and returns to the client immediately. If not, it **forwards the write to the datum's home region** (a cross-region hop), which applies it and acks. Either way, once committed in the owner, the change is **replicated asynchronously** to the other regions in the background --- so other regions become consistent after the replication lag. The client's subsequent reads are served **locally**, and if they just wrote, I **pin them to the home region** (or have their read wait on the write's version token) so they see their own write despite the local replica lagging. So the write is: route to owner, local commit there, async fan-out, and a read-your-writes guard for the writer.
Int: You said the write forwards to the home region if the local region isn't the owner. Isn't that cross-region hop the latency you went multi-region to avoid?
Only on writes, and only when the writer is away from their data's home --- which is the minority case, because a user's home region is chosen to be where they usually are. Reads (the large majority of traffic) are always local and fast; it's only a remote *write* that pays the hop. If a user's writes are *consistently* remote --- they moved --- then ownership is wrong, and I migrate the datum's home region to track them (a controlled, rare operation, not per-request). So I've localized the common case and paid the cross-region cost only on the rarer remote write, which is the whole point of single-writer ownership: local reads everywhere, conflict-free writes, and the latency tax confined to remote writes I can further minimize by moving the home.
Int2: The write committed in the owner and returned to the client, but hadn't replicated yet when that region died. What did the client experience?
The client got a **success ack for a write that's now lost** --- it's in the replication lag window, so it never reached another region, and on failover the promoted replica doesn't have it. That's my RPO made concrete: the data lost is bounded by lag times write-rate. This is exactly why I state an explicit RPO and *alarm* on lag, and why for truly no-loss data (a payment) I'd replicate that slice synchronously or semi-synchronously so the ack only returns once a second region has it --- accepting the ~100ms latency for that slice. For everything else, I accept a small, measured data-loss window rather than taxing every write, and I'm honest with the business that "acknowledged" means "durable in one region, replicated within my RPO," not "durable everywhere instantly."

### DESIGN | "Add disaster recovery to a business-critical single-region system. Target RTO 15 minutes, RPO 1 minute."

Task: Right-size the topology, the replication, and the failover to hit 15-and-1 --- and say how you'd prove it.
Model: This is **DR, not latency**, so active-passive is the right, simpler topology --- a standby in a second region, not active-active with its multi-writer complexity. For **RPO 1 minute**, I replicate the data layer asynchronously with sub-minute lag, monitored with an alarm if lag exceeds a minute. For **RTO 15 minutes**, the standby must be at least **warm** (or pilot-light with fast scale-up) --- data always replicating and compute pre-provisioned or rapidly scalable, because a cold backup-restore can't recover in 15 minutes. Failover: multi-vantage health checks detect a real region outage conservatively, a global routing layer or low-TTL DNS shifts traffic, and the standby's replica is promoted --- with the **old primary fenced before promotion**. Then I run scheduled **game days** that actually execute the failover and measure real RTO and RPO against the targets, adjusting standby warmth and replication lag until they're met.
Int: Why not active-active, since it fails over faster?
Because the requirement is DR, not global latency, and active-active carries the full multi-writer conflict and consistency complexity --- and roughly double the running cost --- for benefits (low global latency, no idle capacity) the requirement doesn't ask for. Active-passive meets a 15-minute RTO and 1-minute RPO far more simply: one writer, no conflicts, a clean promote-the-standby model. It's the right-sized solution. I'd only move to active-active if a latency or near-zero-RTO requirement emerged that active-passive genuinely couldn't meet --- adding complexity to buy a benefit nobody asked for is how you fail the "judgment" bar.
Int2: Your game day six months ago hit the targets. Why isn't that enough?
Because DR rots as the system changes around it, so a six-month-old pass certifies a system that no longer exists. Since then we've added services, changed configs, rotated secrets, grown data volume, and maybe edited the failover scripts --- any of which can silently break the standby: a service deployed only to the primary, a secret not replicated, a schema change the replica can't apply, lag creeping above the RPO. So DR testing has to be regular and automated, not a one-time certification --- I re-run game days on a cadence so the plan is verified against the *current* system. DR is a freshness-sensitive guarantee: it's only as good as its last successful, representative test.

### FAILURE | "Your primary region just went dark --- health checks are failing across the board. Walk the incident."

Task: Walk the incident --- confirm, contain, fail over safely, recover.
Model: **Confirm, fence, promote, verify, then fail back carefully.** First I **confirm it's really down, not just unreachable from one vantage point** --- multiple failed checks from multiple vantage points on a real request path --- because a false failover is an outage I'd cause myself, and if the primary is actually alive I'm about to risk split-brain. Once confirmed, I **fence the old primary** (revoke its write ability via a token the storage layer enforces) *before* promoting, so even if it's alive-but-partitioned it can't keep writing. Then I **promote the standby**: drain its replication backlog, flip it to writable, warm any cold capacity, and **shift traffic** at the routing layer (anycast/global-LB for speed, not just DNS). I **verify** it's serving correct traffic, and I accept that I've lost up to my RPO of writes. Later I **fail back carefully** --- re-establish the old region as a replica of the new primary, sync it, and switch back in a controlled window, not in a rush that causes a second outage.
Int: You promoted the standby. A few minutes later the "dead" primary comes back and starts serving the users who could still reach it. What now?
This is the split-brain scenario, and whether it's a disaster depends entirely on whether I fenced. **If I fenced before promoting**, the returning primary's writes are rejected at the storage layer --- its token is now stale --- so it can serve *reads* to those users but cannot corrupt data by writing, and I bring it back cleanly as a replica of the new primary. **If I didn't fence**, I now have two primaries taking writes, their histories are diverging, and on reconciliation I have conflicting "committed" writes with no single truth --- which is why fencing is non-negotiable and must happen *before* promotion, not after. So the answer is: because I fenced, the returning primary can't write, and I re-attach it as a replica; the incident is contained, not doubled. The lesson the interviewer is probing is whether I understand that "the primary came back" is only catastrophic if I promoted without fencing.
Int2: During the failover, users report they lost data they'd just entered. Is that a bug?
Not necessarily a bug --- it may be my RPO working as designed. Async replication means the last ~minute of writes (my RPO window) hadn't reached the standby when the primary died, so on promotion those writes are gone: the users who wrote in that window lost that data, exactly as the RPO predicted. That's the cost I signed up for by choosing async over sync. What I'd check is whether the loss is *within* the RPO (expected, and I communicate it) or *beyond* it (a real bug --- replication was lagging above the RPO and my alarm should have fired, or the promotion didn't drain the backlog it did have). So I distinguish "RPO-bounded loss, which is the design" from "loss beyond RPO, which is an incident" --- and if it's the former, the fix isn't at failover time, it's deciding whether that data needed synchronous replication in the first place.

### CURVEBALL | Split-brain | "Your monitoring says the primary is down and automation fails over. But the primary was only partitioned from your monitoring, and is still happily serving a chunk of users. Your system is now 'working.' Fix it."

Task: Reframe the premise, then give the real mechanism.
Model: The premise to say aloud: the automation did what it was told, but **"down" and "unreachable from my monitor" are indistinguishable from where the monitor sits** --- a partition looks exactly like a failure, so I failed over on ambiguous evidence and now have two live primaries. That's **split-brain**, and the writes both sides are taking are diverging irreconcilably. The real fix is structural and it's about **enforcement, not detection**: I only ever promote after **fencing** the old primary --- every write carries a monotonic token, and the storage layer rejects any write bearing a token older than the highest it's seen, so when the new primary takes a higher token, the old primary's writes are rejected even though it's alive and I can't reach it. The alternative is a **quorum** model where the partitioned-off minority can't form a majority and stops writing on its own. Either way, at most one region can write a given datum at any instant, so a partition can't cause divergence.
Int: Fencing needs to revoke the old primary's writes --- but it's partitioned, you can't reach it. How does that actually work?
That's the elegant part: fencing works at the **resource the writes land on**, not at the isolated node, so I never have to reach the partitioned primary. Every write carries a monotonically increasing fencing token, and the shared resource --- the database, the storage layer, the log --- rejects any write with a token lower than the highest it has already accepted. When I promote the new primary, I issue it a *higher* token; the instant it writes, the resource's watermark advances, and the old primary's writes (still carrying the old, now-stale token) are refused --- even though it's alive and thinks it's in charge. The lease variant is the same idea in time: the primary holds a time-bounded lease it must renew, so a partitioned primary's lease simply expires and it stops being allowed to write, while the new primary takes a fresh lease only after the old one is guaranteed dead. So fencing is enforcement at the write target, which is exactly why it defends against a node you can't even talk to.

### CLOSE | "Sum it up --- and what's the one thing you'd alarm on in prod?"

Task: Two-sentence close, then the single most important alarm.
Model: It's a multi-region design driven by physics: I name the motivation (DR, latency, or residency), give each datum a single owning region so reads are local and writes are conflict-free, replicate asynchronously so the lag is my RPO, and prevent split-brain by fencing the old primary before promoting --- with failover I've proven under game days, and the honest default that most systems should just do multi-AZ plus backups. In prod, the one thing I'd alarm on is **replication lag exceeding my RPO** --- because a lag above the RPO means my DR promise is *already* broken and nobody knows, it's the earliest signal that a failover would lose more data than I've committed to, and it's the metric that silently rots while everything looks green.
Int: You've got two weeks, not two months. What do you cut to ship, and what would you never cut?
The sophistication goes, never the correctness. I'd cut down to **active-passive** (skip active-active and the conflict machinery), a **pilot-light** standby (skip a hot duplicate), **async replication with a monitored RPO** (skip any synchronous slice unless there's a truly no-loss requirement), and **manual/assisted failover** (skip full automation) --- all of which I can upgrade later without a rewrite. What I would **never** cut is **fencing before promotion** and a **single-writer guarantee**, because those are the correctness: a failover that promotes without fencing can cause split-brain and corrupt the data, which isn't an MVP, it's an incident. And I'd never cut *at least one* real game day, because an untested DR plan is an assumption, not DR. Knowing which parts are the correctness and which are the polish is the answer.

### Extra Curveballs

### CURVEBALL | Residency | "Your active-active global service must comply with GDPR: EU users' personal data cannot leave the EU. How does that change the design?"

Task: Reframe residency as a placement constraint, then adapt ownership and replication.
Model: Residency turns the design from "replicate everywhere for availability and latency" into a **data-placement constraint**, so I partition data by residency and stop replicating regulated data globally. Concretely: **shard users by home jurisdiction** and store EU users' personal data *only* in EU region(s) --- it lives in and stays in the EU, never replicated to non-EU regions. **Selective replication**: replicate freely the data I may (non-regulated, or non-EU users'), but keep regulated EU data within-jurisdiction, so replication becomes per-dataset policy, not blanket. **DR within jurisdiction**: the standby for EU-resident data must itself be in the EU (a second EU region), because I can't fail EU personal data over to the US. **Residency-aware ownership**: in active-active, a write for an EU user is routed to and owned by an EU region, so it never even transits a non-compliant region. And **global views query per-region and aggregate** (or replicate only a non-regulated projection), so a global feature works without copying personal data out. Residency makes region the unit of data ownership *by law*.
Int: An EU resident travels to the US and uses the app. Where do their writes go, and what crosses the border?
Their writes still go to their **home EU region** --- residency follows the *data subject*, not the request origin, so an EU resident's regulated data stays EU-owned wherever they physically are. What happens is a cross-region hop: the US region their request lands on **forwards** their writes to the EU owner (adding latency for that roaming user, acceptable because it's the exception), and reads of their regulated data come from the EU or are simply slower while abroad. What must *not* cross the border is the regulated payload being *persisted* in the US --- so the US edge terminates the connection and proxies to the EU rather than caching or storing personal data locally. Non-regulated data (a product catalog) can still serve from the US normally. The routing is global; the *storage* of regulated data is pinned --- a roaming user pays latency, not a compliance violation.

### CURVEBALL | Read-your-writes | "A user updates their profile in the EU, then their next request hits a US replica and shows the old value. They think the app is broken. Is this a bug, and how do you fix it?"

Task: Diagnose it as a read-your-writes violation, then the scoped fix.
Model: It's a real bug from the user's perspective, but it's the expected consequence of **async replication**: the write applied in the EU (or the EU-owned datum) hasn't propagated to the US replica the next read hit, so they see stale data --- and it's most jarring because it's *their own* write that vanished. It's a **read-your-writes** violation, and I fix it without making all reads global. The cheap fix: **pin the user's reads to the region that took the write for a short window** after they write, so they read the fresh copy while other replicas catch up. The precise fix: the write returns a **version token / LSN**, the client carries it on subsequent reads, and a replica only answers once it has applied at least that version (otherwise it waits, or the read routes to a fresh-enough replica). Either way I tighten consistency for the *one user who just wrote*, during the brief lag window, rather than paying strong consistency on every read for everyone.
Int: Pinning the user to the write region works --- until that region is far away or fails. What's the more robust fix?
The **version watermark**, because pinning is a heuristic that silently voids on a re-route or a region failure: if the pinned region is far, the user's reads get slow; if it fails, their reads move to a replica that may be *behind* the write, and they travel backwards in time exactly when the system is already degraded. The watermark makes it a real guarantee: instead of trusting stickiness, the read carries the write's LSN and I *choose or wait for* a replica that has applied it --- so a re-route just picks a fresh-enough replica rather than a stale one, and a region failure doesn't reintroduce the stale read. Stickiness becomes just an optimization that usually makes the wait unnecessary. And there's a subtler cousin to watch for --- consistent-prefix, where a reader sees an *effect before its cause* because causally-related writes on different regions replicate at different rates --- which single-writer ownership does *not* fix and needs causal tracking where it's user-visible.

### CURVEBALL | Clock skew | "Your active-active store uses last-write-wins for conflicts. In testing it's fine. In production, users occasionally report an edit they made and confirmed just... reverting. Connect the dots."

Task: Trace it to LWW plus cross-region clock skew, then the fix.
Model: The dots: **LWW decides the winner by timestamp, and across regions those timestamps come from different machines whose clocks skew.** Two concurrent writes to the same datum from two regions each get their local clock's timestamp; LWW keeps the higher one and **silently discards the other**. When region B's clock runs, say, 200ms ahead of A's, B's write "wins" even if A's actually happened *later* in real time --- so a user's genuinely newer edit gets clobbered by an older one, and their change "reverts." Even with NTP, skew of tens of milliseconds is normal, which is the same order as replication lag, so the ordering LWW relies on is not trustworthy. The fix: don't use wall-clock LWW for anything you can't afford to lose. **Avoid the conflict** with single-writer ownership (route that datum's writes to one region, so there's no concurrent write to resolve); where you must multi-write, use **version vectors** to at least *detect* concurrency (so you know a conflict happened rather than silently dropping), **CRDTs** where the data merges losslessly, or a system with **bounded clock uncertainty** (Spanner's TrueTime) if you truly need timestamp ordering.
Int: The team says "we'll just sync the clocks better with NTP." Does that fix it?
It *reduces* the probability but doesn't fix the *class* of bug, so I'd push back. NTP typically leaves single-digit-to-tens of milliseconds of skew, and even PTP leaves microseconds-to-milliseconds --- so you've made the bad window smaller, but for concurrent writes landing within that window, LWW still silently discards one, and you still can't *prove* which write was truly later. More importantly, "sync the clocks better" treats a **correctness** problem as a tuning problem: the real issue is that LWW has no way to represent "these two writes are concurrent and both matter." The durable fix is to stop depending on wall-clock ordering for data you can't lose --- single-writer ownership so there's no concurrency to resolve, or version vectors that *detect* concurrency and let me merge or surface both writes. Spanner's TrueTime is the exception that proves the rule: it makes timestamp ordering safe not by syncing clocks perfectly but by exposing a *bounded uncertainty* and waiting it out (commit-wait), which is a real engineering cost. So better NTP is a mitigation, not a fix; the fix is not letting a skewed clock silently decide which acknowledged write survives.

### CURVEBALL | DNS failover | "You set a 30-second DNS TTL for fast failover. The primary region dies, you update DNS, and 20 minutes later a large fraction of traffic is *still* hitting the dead region. Explain, and fix your RTO."

Task: Explain why DNS didn't shift traffic, then give the reliable mechanism.
Model: The premise to correct: a low TTL is a *request*, not a guarantee, because **you don't control the resolvers and clients caching your record**. Some ISP and corporate resolvers **clamp low TTLs** to a minimum and cache for minutes regardless; browsers and OSes do their own **DNS pinning** beyond the TTL; and long-lived **connections** (an open HTTP/2 stream, a database connection pool) don't re-resolve at all --- they keep talking to the dead region's IP until the connection breaks. So even at a 30-second TTL, a meaningful, un-boundable tail keeps hitting the failed region, and my *effective* RTO for those clients is far worse than my TTL suggests. The fix for a tight, reliable RTO is to **not move traffic with DNS**: use **anycast** (the same IP announced from multiple regions; withdrawing the dead region's BGP route reroutes clients at the network layer in seconds) or a **global load balancer** (the edge stops routing to the unhealthy region, and it owns the client connection so it can fail the backend over transparently). DNS is fine for the coarse "which region" decision; it's the wrong tool for fast failover.
Int: Even with anycast, a user with an open connection to the dead region --- what actually gets them onto a healthy region?
Their **existing connection has to break and a new one has to be established** --- routing changes alone don't rescue a connection already pinned to a dead endpoint. So: the client (or the edge in front of it) detects the failure (a timeout or reset), **tears down** the dead connection, and **reconnects** --- at which point anycast routes the new connection to a surviving region. For this to be fast and graceful, the responsibility usually sits at a **global load balancer or edge proxy** that terminates the client connection near the user and maintains its *own* connections to regional backends, so when a backend region dies the edge fails those over transparently and the client's connection to the edge survives. Absent that, I need client-side resilience --- sensible timeouts, retry-with-backoff on connection failure, and re-resolution on reconnect. And any session state from the dead region has to be reconstructable from replicated/owned data in the survivor. So recovery for an in-flight user is teardown-plus-reconnect-plus-session-continuity, which is why an edge tier that owns the connection, plus replicated session data, is what makes failover feel seamless --- not DNS.

### CURVEBALL | Failback | "You failed over cleanly to the secondary and it's serving fine. The original region recovers. A junior engineer wants to 'switch back to primary now.' What's your concern?"

Task: Name the failback hazards, then the safe procedure.
Model: My concern is that **failback is a second failover, with two specific hazards: a stale primary and a fresh split-brain.** The recovered region has been *offline*, so its data is **stale** --- it's missing every write the secondary took while it was down --- so switching back to it as-is would lose all that data (a huge, self-inflicted RPO) and possibly conflict with the current truth. And doing it "now," in a rush, risks the same split-brain as any promotion: if I make the old region writable while the secondary is still the real primary, I have two writers. The safe procedure: first **re-establish the old region as a replica of the current primary (the secondary)** and let it **catch up** --- replay everything it missed until its lag is near zero. Only then, in a **controlled, low-traffic window**, do I fail back: fence the current primary, promote the caught-up old region, shift traffic --- the same disciplined detect/fence/promote I used to fail over. And I'd ask *why* switch back at all: if the secondary is serving fine, there's often no urgency, and a rushed failback just doubles my exposure to failover risk for no benefit.
Int: Why not just switch back immediately --- the primary is our "main" region, after all?
Because "main" is an operational label, not a technical reason, and treating it as one is how you cause an outage recovering from an outage. The secondary is serving correctly *right now*; the recovered primary is **stale and unexercised** (it just came back from a failure and hasn't held live traffic since). Switching back immediately means either losing the writes the secondary took while primary was down (if I don't sync first) or risking split-brain (if I make it writable before fencing the secondary) --- both to satisfy a preference for which region is "primary," not a requirement. The mature stance is that **failback is scheduled, not reflexive**: I only fail back after the old region has caught up as a replica, and only in a controlled window, and only if there's an actual reason (cost, latency, or capacity) to prefer it over the perfectly-good secondary. Every unnecessary failover --- and failback is one --- is fresh exposure to failover risk, so I don't spend it on a label.

### CURVEBALL | Capacity | "Active-active across two regions, each comfortably at 65% CPU. One region fails and traffic shifts to the survivor as designed. Within minutes the survivor is down too. What happened, and how do you prevent it?"

Task: Diagnose the N-1 capacity failure, then size and defend correctly.
Model: What happened is the **N-1 capacity trap**: each region was at 65%, so when one failed, its traffic landed on the survivor, which now needed 130% of one region's capacity --- more than it has --- so it saturated and fell over, turning a clean single-region loss into a total outage. The "graceful" failover cascaded. The fix is to **provision so that N-1 regions can carry 100% of traffic**: at two regions, each must run at **50% or less**, so a survivor can absorb the other's load and sit at 100%, not 130%. That means buying 2x capacity --- the same effective cost as an idle standby, just spread across regions doing useful work --- and at three regions each can run at ~66% (lose one, the other two go to 100%), so the marginal cost of resilience *falls* as I add regions. I can't rely on autoscaling to save me: it takes minutes exactly when I'm already degraded, the capacity may not be available (a regional event has everyone scaling at once), and the freshly-scaled fleet has cold caches that hammer the database I'm trying to protect.
Int: So active-active's "no idle capacity" advantage over a standby is... not real?
It's real but often overstated, and the honest framing matters. Active-active's spare capacity is **doing useful work** (serving traffic) instead of sitting dark like an idle standby, so it's *better utilized* --- that part is true. But the *amount* of spare capacity you must buy is the same: to survive a region loss, you need N-1 regions able to carry 100%, which at two regions is 2x total capacity --- identical to running a full standby. So active-active doesn't let you buy *less* capacity for the same resilience; it lets you *use* the resilience capacity productively in the meantime, and it fails over faster (less to promote). The trap is a team that hears "no idle capacity" and runs each region hot (65%+) to save money, which silently removes the N-1 headroom and guarantees the survivor collapses on failover. So the discipline is: size for N-1 first, *then* enjoy that the headroom is doing useful work --- never run so hot that a region loss overloads the survivors.

### Frames

- Multi-region for **DR / latency / residency** (each pulls the design differently); NOT the same as multi-AZ (a whole-region blast radius vs a data-center one) --- and most systems only need multi-AZ.
- Topology = **active-passive** (DR: simple, idle, failover delay) vs **active-active** (latency: no idle, conflicts); **read-local/write-global** (single owner per datum) is the conflict-free middle; cross-region data = **async** (lag = RPO) by default, sync only for the no-loss slice (~100ms/write).
- Physics forces async -> accept an RPO and possible conflicts; commit to **explicit RTO/RPO** and alarm on lag; prevent **split-brain** with single-writer / quorum / **fence-before-promote**; route and fail over at a global layer (anycast/global-LB fast, geo-DNS TTL-bound); **prove it with game days**; and prefer multi-AZ + backups unless a real requirement forces multi-region.
